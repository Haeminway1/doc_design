Reading — AI Ethics 1
Passage 1 2024 한양대학교 편입 (독해)

As artificial intelligence systems become embedded in consequential decisions—hiring, lending, criminal sentencing—the question of algorithmic bias has moved from academic debate to urgent public concern. These systems learn from historical data that often encodes past discrimination, reproducing and sometimes amplifying social inequities at scale. A model trained on decades of loan records, for instance, may systematically disadvantage applicants from minority communities not because of explicit prejudice in its code, but because of patterns inherited from an unequal past.

Addressing this challenge requires more than technical fixes. It demands institutional accountability—clear lines of responsibility for outcomes that AI systems produce, robust audit mechanisms, and meaningful redress for those harmed. Without enforceable standards, the deployment of AI risks laundering historical injustice through a veneer of mathematical objectivity, lending false legitimacy to outcomes that perpetuate inequality.
