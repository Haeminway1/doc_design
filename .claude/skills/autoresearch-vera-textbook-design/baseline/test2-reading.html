Passage 1 2024 한양대학교 편입학시험

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has forced societies to confront a fundamental tension: the pursuit of efficiency and the preservation of human dignity. As AI systems increasingly influence decisions in hiring, lending, and criminal justice, critics argue that these technologies risk embedding existing social biases into automated processes, effectively laundering discrimination through the veneer of objectivity.

Proponents of algorithmic governance contend that machine-driven decisions eliminate the arbitrary prejudices of individual human judgment. Yet research has consistently demonstrated that training data drawn from historically unequal societies will perpetuate those inequalities rather than correct them. When a hiring algorithm is trained on decades of employment records reflecting systemic exclusion, the model does not simply mirror the past — it codifies it as a blueprint for the future.

This challenge has prompted a growing movement among ethicists and regulators demanding algorithmic accountability — the principle that automated systems must be auditable, contestable, and subject to meaningful human oversight. The debate ultimately reveals a deeper question: whether the design of AI systems is a technical problem to be optimized, or a moral one that demands ongoing deliberation about the values a society chooses to encode in its tools.
