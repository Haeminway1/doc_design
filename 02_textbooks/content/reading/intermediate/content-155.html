The "simulation hypothesis," proposed by philosopher Nick Bostrom, argues that it is statistically probable that we are living in a computer simulation created by a more advanced civilization. The argument rests on three premises, one of which must be true: (1) civilizations almost always go extinct before reaching a "post-human" stage capable of running such simulations; (2) posthuman civilizations are almost never interested in running ancestor-simulations; or (3) we are almost certainly living in a simulation. ① Bostrom argues that if (1) and (2) are false, then a vast number of ancestor-simulations would be created by posthuman civilizations. ② Since the number of simulated beings would vastly outnumber the non-simulated ones, the probability that any given individual (like us) is one of the simulated ones is extremely high. ③ This argument provides definitive physical proof that our reality is not the base reality. ④ The hypothesis is not a claim about certainty but a probabilistic argument about our epistemic position. ⑤ It forces a radical re-evaluation of our assumptions about reality, consciousness, and our place in the cosmos.
Which sentence mischaracterizes the nature and conclusion of Bostrom's simulation argument?
  • (1) ①
  • (2) ②
  • (3) ③
  • (4) ④
  • (5) ⑤
