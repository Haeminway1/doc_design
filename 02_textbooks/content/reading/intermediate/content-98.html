The "simulacrum," as theorized by Jean Baudrillard, represents the final stage in the evolution of the image in postmodern society. Baudrillard outlines four stages: first, the image is a reflection of a basic reality. Second, it masks and perverts a basic reality. Third, it masks the absence of a basic reality. (A)____________, in the final stage, the image bears no relation to any reality whatsoever; it is its own pure simulacrum. At this point, the distinction between the real and the representation implodes. The simulacrum is not a copy of the real, but becomes truth in its own right—a hyperreality. (B)____________, Disneyland is presented as an "imaginary" world to make us believe that the world outside, America itself, is "real." For Baudrillard, this is a deception; America is also a hyperreal simulation.
Which of the following best completes (A) and (B)?

  • (2) Similarly / However
  • (3) Finally / For instance
  • (4) In addition / Therefore
  • (5) However / In conclusion

The "no-fly zone" paradox in military intervention presents a significant ethical and strategic dilemma. A no-fly zone is often proposed as a limited, humanitarian intervention to protect civilians from aerial bombardment without committing to a full-scale ground war. It appears to be a moderate, low-risk option. ________________________________________. To enforce a no-fly zone, a nation or coalition must be willing and able to destroy the target country's air defenses and shoot down any aircraft that violate the zone. This requires a significant military commitment and constitutes a clear act of war, carrying the risk of escalation and retaliation. Therefore, the supposedly "limited" intervention of a no-fly zone is, in practice, a major military undertaking with profound and often unpredictable consequences.
