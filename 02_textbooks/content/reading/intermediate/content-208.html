The "gift," as analyzed by anthropologist Marcel Mauss, is a social phenomenon that challenges the modern economic distinction between self-interest and altruism. In his classic work, *The Gift*, Mauss studied exchange practices in archaic societies and found that gifts are never truly "free." They are part of a complex system of "total services" that are at once voluntary and obligatory, interested and disinterested. The giving of a gift creates a powerful social bond and places an obligation on the recipient to reciprocate at a later time. To refuse a gift or to fail to reciprocate is to sever the social relationship and risk hostility. The "state of exception," as theorized by Carl Schmitt and later elaborated by Giorgio Agamben, refers to a situation in which the sovereign authority suspends the normal legal order in response to a perceived emergency or threat. In this state, the law is paradoxically set aside by the law itself. The sovereign decides that the normal rules no longer apply, creating a zone of indistinction between law and violence. This is not a state of simple anarchy; rather, it is a legal framework for what is outside the law. Schmitt famously argued that the essence of sovereignty lies precisely in this power: "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception." This concept challenges liberal theories of law that see it as a stable and universally applicable system.
