( ① ) He posits a historical progression of the image, beginning with the image as a reflection of a basic reality. ( ② ) Subsequently, the image masks and perverts reality, and then it masks the absence of reality. ( ③ ) In the final stage, however, the image bears no relation to any reality whatsoever; it is its own pure simulacrum. ( ⑤ ) In this state, there is no longer an original reality to appeal to; we are left only with the endless play of self-referential signs.

In his seminal work *The Souls of Black Folk*, W. E. B. Du Bois articulated the unique psychological challenge faced by African Americans. ( ① ) He argued that the Black experience in America is defined by a peculiar "twoness." ( ② ) It is the feeling of being both an American and a Negro; two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings warring in one dark body. ( ③ ) This internal conflict is not a personal failing but a direct result of living within a racist society that refuses to grant full humanity and citizenship. ( ④ ) The individual is thus forced to see themselves not as a unified self, but through the distorting lens of a dominant culture that has defined them as "a problem." ( ⑤ ) This concept has since become a foundational tool for understanding the internal experience of marginalized groups.
