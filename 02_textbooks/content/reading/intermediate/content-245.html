The "uncanny valley" is a hypothesis concerning the emotional response of humans to robots or other non-human entities. The theory posits that as a robot's appearance is made more human-like, an observer's emotional response becomes increasingly positive, but only up to a point. ( ① ) When the robot becomes "almost human" but has subtle flaws that betray its artificial nature, the emotional response abruptly shifts to strong revulsion. ( ② ) This sharp dip in affinity is the "uncanny valley." ( ③ ) The unsettling feeling is often attributed to the perception of a diseased or deceased human, or to a cognitive dissonance between the human-like appearance and the lack of a genuine human essence. ( ④ ) Once the robot's appearance becomes virtually indistinguishable from a healthy human, the emotional response becomes positive once again. ( ⑤ ) This hypothesis has significant implications for designers of robots and animated characters, suggesting they should either aim for a clearly artificial look or achieve near-perfect human realism.
