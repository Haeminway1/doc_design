The post-structuralist critique of language, particularly in the work of thinkers like Claude Lévi-Strauss and Jacques Lacan, challenges the idea of a stable, one-to-one correspondence between a signifier (a word or image) and a signified (a concept). ( ① ) They argue that meaning is not inherent in signs but is produced through their relationships and differences within a larger system. ( ② ) In some cases, certain signifiers become overloaded with symbolic meaning yet remain fundamentally empty and open to interpretation. ( ③ ) Concepts like "freedom," "justice," or "the people" often function in this way in political discourse. ( ④ ) Their very ambiguity allows them to serve as powerful rallying points, uniting diverse groups under a single banner, even if each group understands the term differently. ( ⑤ ) The struggle over the meaning of these key terms is therefore a central aspect of political and ideological conflict.

In his critique of modern consumer society, Herbert Marcuse argued that the apparent liberation of instincts, particularly sexuality, was not a genuine form of freedom. ( ① ) He observed that in the post-war era, capitalist society seemed to have become more permissive, with sexuality openly displayed and commercialized in advertising and media. ( ② ) However, he saw this not as a subversion of the established order but as a new, more subtle form of social control. ( ③ ) By channeling libidinal energy into the consumption of commodities, the system neutralizes its potentially revolutionary force. ( ④ ) The satisfaction offered is managed and controlled, preventing the emergence of a genuine opposition that would demand a qualitative transformation of society. ( ⑤ ) Individuals are thus pacified by a controlled pleasure, becoming content with their servitude.
