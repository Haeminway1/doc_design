The "iron cage" is a term used by sociologist Max Weber to describe the increased rationalization and bureaucratization of modern life. Weber argued that while these rational systems are highly efficient, they trap individuals in an impersonal, rule-governed reality. In this "cage," social action is increasingly based on efficiency and calculation rather than on traditional values or emotions. The pursuit of wealth, for example, becomes a purely mechanical and meaningless end in itself, stripped of the ethical and religious significance it may have once held. This process leads to a profound "disenchantment of the world," where life is devoid of mystery and spiritual meaning. Weber was deeply ambivalent about this development, recognizing the material benefits of rationalization while lamenting its cost to the human spirit.

The "double consciousness," a term coined by W. E. B. Du Bois, describes a sense of internal conflict among subordinated groups in an oppressive society. Du Bois first used it to describe the experience of African Americans, who must always view themselves through the dual lens of their own identity and the perspective of the dominant white society. He described it as a "sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others." This creates a "twoness," a feeling of being both an American and a Negro, two unreconciled souls warring in one body. This is not a personal psychological failing but a social product of racism. The concept has since been applied to understand the internal experience of other marginalized groups, such as women and postcolonial subjects. It remains a powerful tool for analyzing the psychological effects of social inequality.
