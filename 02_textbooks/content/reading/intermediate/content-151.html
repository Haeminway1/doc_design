The "subaltern," in postcolonial theory, refers to social groups who are excluded and displaced from the dominant power structures of a society. The term, adopted by Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak from the work of Antonio Gramsci, designates those whose voices are not heard in the historical record. ① Spivak's seminal essay asks, "Can the Subaltern Speak?" ② Her answer is a complex and pessimistic "no." ③ This is not because the subaltern lacks the capacity for speech, but because the structures of power—both colonial and patriarchal—do not provide a space for that speech to be heard and recognized as legitimate. ④ Any attempt by the subaltern to speak within these structures is inevitably co-opted or misinterpreted. ⑤ Therefore, the primary task of the postcolonial intellectual is to accurately represent and speak on behalf of the subaltern, giving voice to their silent experiences.

The "hard problem of consciousness," a term coined by David Chalmers, refers to the question of why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective, qualitative experience, or "qualia." This is contrasted with the "easy problems," which concern the brain's functional and behavioral abilities, such as processing information or controlling attention.
