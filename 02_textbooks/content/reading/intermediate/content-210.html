The "general intellect," a concept from Karl Marx's *Grundrisse*, refers to the collective knowledge, social intelligence, and scientific labor that becomes a primary productive force in advanced capitalism. Marx predicted a stage where the creation of wealth would depend less on the direct labor time of individual workers and more on the power of knowledge and technology. In this stage, the accumulated knowledge of society—science, technology, communication networks—becomes a "general intellect" that is objectified in the fixed capital of the production system. This creates a contradiction. While this general intellect is a collective and social product, it is privately appropriated by capital. The "molecular," in the philosophy of Deleuze and Guattari, is a concept used to describe a level of reality characterized by fluid, partial, and sub-individual connections and flows. It is contrasted with the "molar," which refers to the large-scale, rigid, and organized structures of society, such as the state, the family, or the individual subject. The molar level is one of clear identities and binary oppositions. The molecular level, however, is a realm of micro-politics, where desires, affects, and particles connect and disconnect in unpredictable ways, constantly forming and dissolving temporary assemblages. These two levels are not separate worlds; they are intertwined. Large molar structures are constantly being undermined or reconfigured by molecular flows from below, and molecular flows are constantly being captured and stratified by molar organizations.
