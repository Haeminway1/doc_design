The "aporia," in deconstructive literary theory, refers to a point of undecidability or internal contradiction within a text. It is a logical impasse where the text seems to undermine its own foundational assumptions. For a deconstructionist critic, these aporias are not flaws to be resolved or ignored. On the contrary, they are the most crucial sites for analysis. By focusing on these moments of irresolvable tension, the critic can unravel the hierarchical oppositions (such as speech/writing or nature/culture) that structure the text. The goal is not to arrive at a single, unified meaning, but to demonstrate how the text is always already "at war with itself." The concept of "standpoint epistemology," particularly in its feminist formulations, challenges the traditional ideal of a neutral, disembodied, and objective knower. It argues that knowledge is always situated and that one's social position—shaped by factors like gender, race, and class—fundamentally affects what one can know. The theory posits that marginalized groups, because of their subordinate position, are not inherently more biased but can potentially achieve a more complete and less distorted understanding of the social world. The dominant group's perspective tends to take the existing power structures for granted, rendering them invisible. The marginalized, however, must understand both their own reality and the reality of the dominant group in order to navigate their lives. This "double vision" can provide the basis for a more critical and comprehensive perspective, but only if it is coupled with collective political struggle and critical reflection.
