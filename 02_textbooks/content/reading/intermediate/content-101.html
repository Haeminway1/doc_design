The "tragedy of the commons" is an economic dilemma where individual users of a shared resource, acting in their own rational self-interest, collectively deplete or spoil that resource. The concept, popularized by Garrett Hardin, uses the analogy of a pasture open to all herders. (A)____________, each herder has an incentive to add more cattle to their herd, as they receive the full benefit of the extra animal while the cost of overgrazing is shared by all. (B)____________, the cumulative effect of these individually rational decisions is the degradation and eventual destruction of the shared pasture. This logic applies to numerous environmental problems, such as overfishing and air pollution. Solving the tragedy typically requires mechanisms like regulation or privatization to align individual incentives with the collective good.

The "Ship of Theseus" is a classic thought experiment in metaphysics that raises profound questions about identity and persistence through change. The paradox imagines the ship of the hero Theseus, which is preserved by the Athenians by gradually replacing its old, decaying planks with new ones. Eventually, a point is reached where no original plank remains. ________________________________________. To complicate the puzzle, imagine that someone collected all the discarded original planks and reassembled them. Now there are two ships: the restored one and the reassembled one. Which is the true Ship of Theseus? The problem forces us to question what constitutes identity—is it the material components, the form, the history, or something else entirely?
