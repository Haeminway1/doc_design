The "bystander effect" is a social psychological phenomenon where individuals are less likely to offer help to a victim when other people are present. ① The greater the number of bystanders, the less likely it is that any one of them will help. ② This is largely due to a "diffusion of responsibility," where each individual feels less personal obligation to act because they assume someone else will or should intervene. ③ Another contributing factor is "pluralistic ignorance," where everyone looks to others for cues on how to react, and if no one is acting, they interpret the situation as a non-emergency. ④ The Asch conformity experiments demonstrated that people will often conform to a group's incorrect answer, even when the correct answer is obvious. ⑤ Overcoming the bystander effect requires a conscious decision to take personal responsibility, recognizing that in a crowd, no one may act unless you do.

The "halo effect" is a type of cognitive bias in which our overall impression of a person influences how we feel and think about their specific character traits. ① For example, if we perceive a person to be attractive, we are also more likely to believe they are intelligent, kind, and successful, even without any direct evidence for these qualities. ② This "halo" of a single positive trait spreads to color our judgment of their other, unrelated traits. ③ This effect works in the reverse as well, a phenomenon sometimes called the "horn effect," where a single negative trait can cause us to assume a person has other negative qualities. ④ The halo effect is a powerful force in many areas, from job interviews and performance reviews to marketing and politics. ⑤ It demonstrates our tendency to make quick, holistic judgments and the difficulty of assessing individual traits independently of our overall impression.
