The "looking-glass self," a concept developed by sociologist Charles Horton Cooley, describes the process by which our sense of self is derived from our perception of how others see us. It is a social-psychological concept that posits the self is not an autonomous entity but is constructed through social interaction. The process has three main components. (A)____________, we imagine our appearance to the other person. Second, we imagine their judgment of that appearance. (B)____________, we experience some sort of self-feeling, such as pride or shame, as a result of that perceived judgment. Our identity is thus a reflection of the judgments we attribute to others, whether those attributions are accurate or not.

The "availability heuristic" is a mental shortcut that relies on immediate examples that come to mind when evaluating a specific topic or decision. This cognitive bias means we tend to overestimate the likelihood of events that are more "available" in our memory, which can be influenced by how recent, frequent, or emotionally charged our memory of them is. ________________________________________. After seeing extensive news coverage of a shark attack, one might develop an exaggerated fear of swimming in the ocean, even though the statistical probability of such an event is extremely low. The vivid and easily recalled image of the attack overrides the more abstract statistical data. While often efficient, this heuristic can lead to systematic errors in judgment and risk assessment.
