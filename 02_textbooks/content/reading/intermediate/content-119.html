Phenomenology, as founded by Edmund Husserl, represents a radical attempt to return "to the things themselves" by analyzing the structures of conscious experience. ( ① ) To achieve a rigorous, presuppositionless science of consciousness, Husserl proposed a specific methodological procedure. ( ② ) He argued that we must set aside our "natural attitude," which is the common-sense belief in the existence of an independent, external world. ( ③ ) The goal is to redirect our focus away from the objects of the world as they are assumed to be, and toward the very acts of consciousness in which these objects appear to us. ( ④ ) By doing so, the phenomenologist can describe the essential features of phenomena as they are given in pure consciousness, without any theoretical or scientific assumptions. ( ⑤ ) This allows for a detailed investigation of how the world is constituted through our intentional acts.

Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari use the botanical metaphor of the "rhizome" to propose a model of thought that opposes traditional, hierarchical structures. ( ① ) They contrast the rhizome with the "arborescent" or tree-like model of knowledge. ( ② ) This traditional model is characterized by a central root (a foundation), a linear progression of trunk and branches, and a predetermined order. ( ③ ) It operates on principles of causality and logical succession, reflecting a centralized and stratified way of thinking. ( ④ ) A book, for instance, can be thought of not as a linear progression from beginning to end, but as a rhizome, a multiplicity of connections that can be entered and explored from any point. ( ⑤ ) This model encourages a "nomadic" thought that makes unforeseen connections and resists being captured by any single, overarching structure.
