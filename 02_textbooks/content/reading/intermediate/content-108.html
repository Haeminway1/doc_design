The "precautionary principle" is a guiding framework for policy-making in situations of scientific uncertainty. It posits that if an action or policy has a suspected risk of causing severe and irreversible harm to the public or the environment, the absence of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent it. In essence, it shifts the burden of proof. ________________________________________. Instead of regulators having to prove that a substance is harmful before restricting it, the proponents of the new technology or chemical must prove that it is safe before it is introduced. This principle is often invoked in debates over climate change, genetic modification, and the regulation of new chemicals, prioritizing caution in the face of potentially catastrophic risks.
