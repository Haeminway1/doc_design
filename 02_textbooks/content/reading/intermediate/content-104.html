The "poverty of the stimulus" is an argument from linguistics, most famously associated with Noam Chomsky, which posits that the linguistic input children receive is insufficient to explain the rich and complex linguistic knowledge they ultimately attain. The concept of "mimetic desire," as developed by literary theorist and philosopher René Girard, posits that most human desire is not direct but is imitated from the desire of others. We desire an object not because of its intrinsic qualities, but because another person—a "mediator" or "model"—desires it. This makes desire triangular: it flows from the subject to the object through the model. ________________________________________. When the subject and the model desire the same object, and the object cannot be shared, the model inevitably becomes a rival and an obstacle. This mimetic rivalry, Girard argues, is the root cause of most human conflict, from personal jealousy to large-scale violence. Society, in his view, is a complex system of rituals and prohibitions designed to contain the destructive potential of mimetic conflict.
