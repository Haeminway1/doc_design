The concept of *différance*, a neologism coined by Jacques Derrida, is central to deconstruction. It deliberately plays on the dual meaning of the French verb *différer*, which means both "to differ" and "to defer." For Derrida, meaning is never fully present in any single sign or moment but is produced through a complex interplay of difference and deferral. The meaning of a word is determined by its difference from other words in the linguistic system. For example, "cat" has meaning only because it is not "bat," "mat," or "car." Simultaneously, meaning is endlessly deferred; each signifier points to another signifier in an infinite chain, with a final, stable meaning (the "transcendental signified") never being reached. In his analysis of tragedy, Friedrich Nietzsche introduces a fundamental distinction between two cosmic forces, which he names after Greek gods: the Apollonian and the Dionysian. The Apollonian represents the principle of order, reason, harmony, and individuation. It is the world of dreams, appearances, and clear forms, associated with sculpture and the rational structure of the epic. The Dionysian, in contrast, is the principle of chaos, intoxication, ecstatic unity, and the dissolution of the individual. It is a primal, undifferentiated force associated with music and frenzied dance, where the boundaries of the self collapse into a collective whole. For Nietzsche, Greek tragedy at its peak was not the triumph of one over the other.
