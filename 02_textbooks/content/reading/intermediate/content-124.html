Sociologist Zygmunt Bauman argued that contemporary society has transitioned from a "solid" to a "liquid" phase of modernity. ( ① ) The "solid" phase was characterized by stable, long-term structures like the nation-state, the traditional family, and lifelong employment, which provided a reliable frame of reference for individual identity. ( ② ) In contrast, the current era is defined by deregulation, flexibility, and constant change. ( ③ ) In this world, commitments are temporary, identities are projects of constant reinvention, and the only certainty is uncertainty itself. ( ④ ) This condition generates both unprecedented individual freedom and profound anxiety, as the old securities have melted away. ( ⑤ ) Individuals are now responsible for navigating this precarious landscape on their own, constantly adapting to new and unpredictable conditions.

Jean-Jacques Rousseau's political philosophy presents a complex and often controversial theory of freedom and sovereignty. ( ① ) In *The Social Contract*, he posits that for a political society to be legitimate, its laws must be an expression of the "general will." ( ② ) An individual can have a private will that conflicts with the general will, but true freedom consists in conforming to the laws one has prescribed for oneself as a member of the collective. ( ③ ) Therefore, in a rather paradoxical formulation, Rousseau suggests that if a citizen refuses to obey the general will, they can be "forced to be free." ( ④ ) This is because the general will represents their own true, rational will as a citizen, even if their private, selfish desires contradict it. ( ⑤ ) This idea has been both celebrated as a theory of radical democracy and criticized as a potential justification for totalitarianism.
