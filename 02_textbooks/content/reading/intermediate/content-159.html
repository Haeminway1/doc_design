The "Turing test," developed by Alan Turing in 1950, is a test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior equivalent to, or indistinguishable from, that of a human. In the test, a human evaluator engages in a natural language conversation with two other parties, one a human and the other a machine. ① If the evaluator cannot reliably tell the machine from the human, the machine is said to have passed the test. ② Turing proposed that the question "Can machines think?" should be replaced by this more practical, operational test. ③ The test is not designed to determine if the machine has consciousness or subjective awareness. ④ It is a purely behavioral test, concerned only with the machine's ability to successfully mimic human conversational abilities. ⑤ Passing the Turing test is now considered a definitive and universally accepted proof that a machine possesses genuine consciousness.

The "Fermi paradox" refers to the apparent contradiction between the high probability of the existence of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of evidence for, or contact with, such civilizations. The argument is simple: the universe is vast and ancient, containing billions of stars older than our sun. (A) It seems statistically inevitable that intelligent life should have arisen elsewhere, and that at least some of it would have developed interstellar travel. (B) The galaxy, therefore, should be teeming with signs of alien life, from probes and megastructures to radio signals. (C) The paradox lies in the profound and deafening silence we observe from the cosmos. (D) This silence provides conclusive evidence that humanity is the only intelligent species in the entire universe. (E) Numerous potential solutions have been proposed, from the "rare Earth" hypothesis to the "great filter" theory, but the paradox remains a powerful and unresolved question.
