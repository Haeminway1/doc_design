The "Pollyanna principle," or positivity bias, is the psychological tendency for people to remember pleasant items more accurately and frequently than unpleasant ones. The term originates from a novel about a girl who finds something to be glad about in every situation. Research has shown that, on a subconscious level, the mind tends to focus on the optimistic. For example, analysis of language across cultures reveals a universal tendency to use positive words more often than negative words. In memory recall tasks, people consistently remember more positive life experiences than negative ones. This does not mean people are naively optimistic or ignore all threats. Rather, it suggests a subconscious cognitive mechanism that leans toward the positive, which may have evolutionary benefits for resilience and motivation.
