The "dramaturgical analysis" of social interaction, developed by sociologist Erving Goffman, uses the metaphor of theatrical performance to understand human behavior. Goffman posits that in our everyday lives, we are all actors on a stage, performing roles for an audience. We engage in "impression management," carefully crafting our appearance, manner, and speech to present a particular image of ourselves. This involves a distinction between the "front stage," where the performance is given, and the "backstage," where we can relax and prepare. For example, a waiter's polite and deferential behavior toward customers is a front-stage performance, while his candid complaints in the kitchen constitute a backstage reality. For Goffman, the "self" is not a fixed, internal entity but a dramatic effect that emerges from the performance itself.

The "technological singularity" is a hypothetical future point when technological growth becomes uncontrollable and irreversible, resulting in unforeseeable changes to human civilization. The concept is most often associated with the advent of an artificial superintelligence (ASI). The premise is that once an AI is created that can recursively improve its own intelligence, it will trigger an "intelligence explosion." This runaway feedback loop would quickly result in an intelligence far surpassing that of all humanity combined. The consequences of such an event are a matter of intense debate. Proponents envision a utopian future where an ASI could solve all of humanity's most intractable problems, from disease to poverty. Critics, however, warn of a profound existential risk, where a superintelligence with goals misaligned with human values could lead to our extinction.
