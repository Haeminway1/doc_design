The "panopticon," an architectural design conceived by philosopher Jeremy Bentham, serves as a powerful metaphor for modern disciplinary power, as analyzed by Michel Foucault. The design features a central observation tower surrounded by a ring of cells, each backlit so that the inmates are constantly visible to a guard in the tower. (A)____________, the inmates cannot see the guard, and thus never know if they are being watched at any given moment. This uncertainty is the key to the panopticon's efficiency. It compels the inmates to internalize the disciplinary gaze and regulate their own behavior as if they were under constant surveillance. (B)____________, external force becomes largely unnecessary. Foucault argued that this model of power extends beyond the prison to schools, hospitals, and factories, creating a "disciplinary society."

The "observer effect" in quantum physics posits that the act of measuring a system inevitably disturbs it. This is not a matter of clumsy instrumentation but a fundamental feature of reality at the subatomic level. To observe a particle like an electron, one must interact with it, for instance, by bouncing a photon off it. This interaction necessarily alters the particle's properties, such as its momentum. ________________________________________. This principle is closely related to the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, which states that one cannot simultaneously know with perfect accuracy certain pairs of properties, like a particle's position and momentum. The more precisely you measure one, the less precisely you know the other. This fundamentally challenges the classical notion of a detached, objective observer who can view the world without affecting it.
