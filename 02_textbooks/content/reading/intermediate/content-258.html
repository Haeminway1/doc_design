The "looking-glass self," a concept from sociologist Charles Horton Cooley, describes the process by which our sense of self is derived from our perception of how others see us. It posits that the self is not an autonomous entity but is constructed through social interaction. The process has three main components: first, we imagine our appearance to the other person; second, we imagine their judgment of that appearance; and third, we experience some sort of self-feeling, such as pride or shame, as a result of that perceived judgment. ________________________________________. It is a reflection of the judgments we attribute to others, whether those attributions are accurate or not. This theory highlights the fundamentally social nature of our identity.
