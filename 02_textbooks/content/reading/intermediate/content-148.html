The "Sokal Hoax" of 1996 was a pivotal and controversial event in the so-called "science wars" of the 1990s. Physicist Alan Sokal submitted a deliberately nonsensical article, filled with postmodern jargon and flawed reasoning, to the cultural studies journal *Social Text*. ① The article, titled "Transgressing the Boundaries: Toward a Transformative Hermeneutics of Quantum Gravity," argued that quantum reality is a social and linguistic construct. ② To Sokal's surprise, the journal's editors accepted and published the article without peer review. ③ This demonstrated the high intellectual standards and rigorous fact-checking procedures prevalent in the field of cultural studies at the time. ④ Sokal then revealed his hoax in another publication, stating that his experiment was a critique of what he saw as the intellectual sloppiness and hostility towards science in some areas of the humanities. ⑤ The affair ignited a fierce debate about academic integrity, the nature of truth, and the relationship between the sciences and the humanities.
