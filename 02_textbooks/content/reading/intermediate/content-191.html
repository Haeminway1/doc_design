The "hyperreal," a concept central to the work of Jean Baudrillard, describes a condition in which the distinction between reality and its simulation has collapsed. In this state, the simulation is no longer a copy of the real, but becomes a truth in its own right—a reality more real than real. Baudrillard argues that this is the condition of contemporary, media-saturated societies. He uses Disneyland as a key example. Disneyland is presented as an "imaginary" world in order to make us believe that the "real" America outside its gates is authentic. For Baudrillard, this is the ultimate deception. The function of Disneyland is to conceal the fact that the "real" world is itself a hyperreal simulation, a carefully constructed order of signs.

The "paradox of thrift" is a central concept in Keynesian economics that challenges the classical view of saving. For an individual, saving more money is a virtuous and rational act. However, the paradox arises when everyone in an economy tries to save more at the same time. This collective increase in the propensity to save leads to a decrease in overall consumption and aggregate demand. In response to falling demand, businesses cut back on production and lay off workers, which reduces the total income of the economy. As a result, the total amount of saving in the economy may actually fall, even though each individual intended to save more. This illustrates a "fallacy of composition," where what is true for a part is not necessarily true for the whole.
