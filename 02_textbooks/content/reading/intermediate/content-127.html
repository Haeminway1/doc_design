Apophatic theology, also known as negative theology, represents a profound and ancient approach to contemplating the divine. ① It asserts that God, as an infinite and transcendent being, cannot be adequately described by any finite human concepts or positive attributes. ② Therefore, this theological path attempts to approach God by negation, by systematically stating what God is *not*—not finite, not mortal, not corporeal. ③ This method is prominent in the writings of thinkers like Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite and Moses Maimonides, who argued that every positive predication about God is a form of limitation. ④ In contrast, cataphatic theology, which is more common in mainstream Western Christianity, confidently uses positive terms and analogies to describe God's attributes, such as "God is love" or "God is good." ⑤ The ultimate goal of the apophatic way is not to arrive at an empty void, but to foster a state of reverent silence and intellectual humility before a mystery that transcends all language.

The sociological concept of "habitus," developed by Pierre Bourdieu, serves as a crucial mediating link between social structure and individual agency. ① Habitus refers to the system of embodied dispositions, tendencies, and skills that are acquired through an individual's life experiences, particularly their upbringing and position in the social hierarchy. ② It is not a set of conscious rules but an intuitive "feel for the game," guiding an individual's perceptions, actions, and tastes in a way that often seems natural or personal. ③ This concept allows Bourdieu to explain how social structures are reproduced without resorting to a deterministic model that negates individual freedom. ④ Bourdieu also developed the concept of "cultural capital," which refers to the non-financial social assets, such as education and intellect, that promote social mobility. ⑤ The habitus, therefore, shapes an individual's choices and practices in a way that is consistent with their social origins, thus perpetuating existing class structures over time.
