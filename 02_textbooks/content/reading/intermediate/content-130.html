The historical school of Annales, founded in France in the early 20th century, revolutionized the study of history. ① Dissatisfied with the traditional focus on political events, diplomacy, and "great men," the Annales historians sought to write a more "total" history. ② They turned their attention toward long-term structures (*longue durée*), deep-seated social patterns, and collective mentalities that shape societies over vast stretches of time. ③ To achieve this, they incorporated methods and insights from other disciplines like geography, economics, and sociology, broadening the scope of historical inquiry. ④ The rise of quantitative history, or cliometrics, in the United States during the 1960s also emphasized economic models and statistical analysis. ⑤ By analyzing subjects like climate, demography, and popular culture, the Annales school shifted history from a narrative of events to an analysis of structures, fundamentally changing what it means to study the past.

The "correspondence bias," also known as the fundamental attribution error, is a pervasive cognitive tendency to over-emphasize dispositional or personality-based explanations for others' behaviors while under-emphasizing situational explanations. ① In other words, we are more likely to attribute someone's actions to their character rather than to the circumstances they are in. ② For example, if we see someone trip and fall, we are more likely to think they are clumsy (a dispositional attribution) than to consider that the pavement might be uneven (a situational attribution). ③ This bias is particularly strong when we are judging the negative behaviors of others. ④ Interestingly, when explaining our own behavior, we often do the opposite, attributing our successes to our character and our failures to the situation, a phenomenon known as the self-serving bias. ⑤ This tendency to overlook the power of the situation can lead to significant misunderstandings and unfair judgments in our social interactions.
