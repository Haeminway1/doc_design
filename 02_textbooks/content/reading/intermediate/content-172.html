The concept of "governmentality," as introduced by Michel Foucault, offers a unique perspective on the nature of modern power. It describes a specific "art of government" that emerged in the 18th century, shifting focus from ruling over a territory to managing a "population." This new form of power, which Foucault termed "biopower," is concerned with the welfare of the populace in all its aspects: its health, wealth, and longevity. To achieve this, it employs a vast array of tactics and knowledge systems, such as statistics and public health initiatives. For instance, the collection of demographic data allows the state to identify trends and intervene in ways that optimize the vitality of the population. This control is often subtle and presented as beneficial, making it a particularly pervasive form of governance. Thus, governmentality reveals how deeply political power is invested in the biological existence of human beings, challenging the traditional separation of politics and life.

The philosophical movement of speculative realism marks a significant departure from the post-Kantian tradition that has dominated continental philosophy. This tradition, which Quentin Meillassoux terms "correlationism," holds that we can only think about the correlation between thought and being, never either term in isolation. In other words, reality independent of the human mind is considered unthinkable. Speculative realism, in its various forms, rejects this anthropocentric constraint. It seeks to reclaim the possibility of thinking about "the great outdoors"—the reality that exists independently of human consciousness. This endeavor has led to a renewed interest in metaphysics and ontology. The movement, therefore, attempts to forge new philosophical tools to access a reality not necessarily made for us, challenging the primacy of human finitude.
