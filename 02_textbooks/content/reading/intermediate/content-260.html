The "Copenhagen interpretation" of quantum mechanics, formulated by Niels Bohr and Werner Heisenberg, is a set of principles that attempt to explain the strange behavior of the subatomic world. A central tenet is that a quantum system does not have definite properties (like a specific position) before it is measured. Instead, it exists in a "superposition" of all possible states, described by a mathematical wave function. The act of measurement forces the system to randomly "choose" one of its possible states, a process known as the "collapse of the wave function." The interpretation famously rejects questions about what the particle was "doing" before the measurement as meaningless. It insists that physics should only be concerned with the results of measurements, as that is all we can ever know. This approach is often summarized as "Shut up and calculate!"
[377]
According to the Copenhagen interpretation, what happens when a measurement is made?
  • (1) The particle's true, pre-existing properties are revealed.
  • (2) The wave function collapses, and the system settles into one definite state.
  • (3) The physicist can determine all of the particle's properties simultaneously.
  • (4) The system enters an even more complex state of superposition.
  • (5) The measurement has no effect on the quantum system.
[378]
The Copenhagen interpretation's attitude towards the "reality" of a particle before measurement can be best described as:
  • (1) certain and deterministic.
  • (2) agnostic and pragmatic.
  • (3) richly detailed and descriptive.
  • (4) completely understood and predictable.
  • (5) identical to the classical physics view.
