The "Sokal hoax" was a scholarly publishing sting that ignited a fierce debate about academic standards in the 1990s. Physicist Alan Sokal submitted a deliberately nonsensical article, laden with postmodern jargon, to the cultural studies journal *Social Text*. The article, titled "Transgressing the Boundaries," argued that physical reality is a social construct. The journal's editors, impressed by its apparent erudition, accepted and published it without sending it for external peer review. Sokal then revealed the hoax, arguing it demonstrated a lack of intellectual rigor and an ideological bias against science in certain humanities circles. The affair became a flashpoint in the "science wars." It raised critical questions about the nature of truth, the role of peer review, and the intellectual responsibilities of scholars in all disciplines.

The "contact hypothesis" in social psychology suggests that direct contact between members of different groups can reduce intergroup prejudice. However, Gordon Allport, who first formalized the theory, stressed that mere contact is not enough; in fact, it can sometimes worsen relations. For contact to be effective, four key conditions must be met: equal status between the groups, common goals, intergroup cooperation, and the support of authorities. When these conditions are fulfilled, contact allows individuals to see each other as individuals rather than stereotypes and to discover common ground. For instance, a well-designed cooperative learning project in a racially mixed school can foster positive relationships. This theory has provided a powerful framework for designing interventions in schools, workplaces, and post-conflict societies to promote tolerance and reconciliation.
