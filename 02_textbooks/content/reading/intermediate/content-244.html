The "just-world hypothesis" is a cognitive bias that reflects a belief that the world is fundamentally fair and that people get what they deserve. This belief can be a psychological comfort, providing a sense of order and predictability. However, it can also lead to negative social consequences, most notably the tendency to blame victims for their misfortune. (A) This is because accepting that terrible things can happen to good people randomly is more threatening to one's sense of a just world than assuming the victim must have done something to deserve their fate. (B) For example, a person holding this belief might conclude that a victim of a robbery was careless or that a person living in poverty is lazy. (C) This rationalization protects the believer's worldview, but it does so at the cost of empathy and a clear understanding of systemic factors like inequality or random chance.
