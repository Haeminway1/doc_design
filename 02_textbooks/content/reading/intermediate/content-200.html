The "rhizome" is a key philosophical concept developed by Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari to describe a non-hierarchical and acentered system of thought and reality. They contrast it with the "arborescent" or tree-like model, which is defined by a central root, a linear progression, and a predetermined structure. The rhizome, however, operates differently. Like an underground network of grass roots, it has no central point and can sprout and connect from any of its nodes. It is a model of multiplicity, connection, and heterogeneity. Any point of a rhizome can be connected to any other, and it is capable of ceaseless modification. This concept encourages a "nomadic" thought that resists being captured by a single, overarching structure. The "Cyborg Manifesto" by Donna Haraway is a foundational text in feminist and posthumanist theory. Haraway proposes the cyborg—a hybrid of machine and organism—as a potent political metaphor for breaking down traditional, rigid dualisms. She argues that the boundaries between human and animal, organism and machine, and the physical and non-physical have become irrevocably blurred in the late 20th century. The cyborg is a creature of a post-gender world; it has no origin story in the sense of a Western, patriarchal creation myth. By embracing this hybrid and artificial identity, Haraway suggests, we can move beyond the limiting and often oppressive politics based on naturalized identities like gender, race, and nature.
