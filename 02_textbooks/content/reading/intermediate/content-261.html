The "precautionary principle" is a framework for policy-making in the face of scientific uncertainty. It states that if an action or policy has a suspected risk of causing severe and irreversible harm to the public or the environment, the absence of full scientific certainty should not be used as a reason to postpone cost-effective measures to prevent it. In essence, it shifts the burden of proof. Instead of regulators having to prove that a new chemical or technology is harmful, the proponents of the activity must prove that it is safe. This principle prioritizes caution and the avoidance of catastrophic risk over potential economic benefits. It is often invoked in debates surrounding climate change, genetically modified organisms, and the introduction of new chemicals, where the potential consequences are vast and not fully understood.

What is the core idea of the precautionary principle?
  • (1) Action should only be taken when there is absolute scientific proof of harm.

  • (3) In the face of uncertain but potentially severe risk, it is better to be safe than sorry.
  • (4) The burden of proof should always fall on those who oppose a new technology.

The precautionary principle "shifts the burden of proof" by requiring that:
  • (1) regulators must prove an activity is harmful before stopping it.
  • (2) the public must demonstrate the exact nature of the risk.
  • (3) the proponents of an activity must demonstrate that it is safe.
  • (4) all scientific uncertainty must be eliminated before a decision is made.
  • (5) only proven, risk-free technologies are ever allowed.
