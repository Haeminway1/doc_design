The "priming effect" is a well-documented phenomenon in psychology where exposure to one stimulus influences a response to a subsequent stimulus, without conscious guidance or intention. ① For instance, if a person reads a list of words including "doctor," they will subsequently recognize the word "nurse" more quickly than an unrelated word like "bread." ② This occurs because the concept of "doctor" primes or activates associated concepts in the brain's semantic network, making them more accessible. ③ This effect can also influence behavior; studies have shown that individuals primed with words related to old age (e.g., "Florida," "wrinkle") subsequently walk more slowly down a hallway. ④ The reliability of some of these more dramatic social priming studies has been questioned in recent years due to the replication crisis in psychology. ⑤ The priming effect demonstrates that much of our information processing and behavior is shaped by subtle, unconscious cues in our environment.

The "uncanny," or *das Unheimliche*, as theorized by Sigmund Freud, describes a peculiar and unsettling form of fear that arises when something familiar suddenly becomes strange and alien. ① It is not about outright horror but about a subtle intellectual uncertainty that blurs the line between the real and the imaginary. ② Freud traced this feeling back to the revival of repressed infantile complexes and surmounted primitive beliefs, such as the idea of a live doll or the return of the dead. ③ The experience occurs when something that was once familiar and homely (*heimlich*) is repressed and then returns from that repression in a distorted form, making it frighteningly unfamiliar (*unheimlich*). ④ The aesthetic concept of the sublime, in contrast, evokes feelings of awe and admiration in the face of overwhelming greatness. ⑤ This psychoanalytic concept has been profoundly influential in literary and film theory for analyzing works that evoke a sense of eerie discomfort and cognitive dissonance.
