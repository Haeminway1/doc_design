The "tragedy of the commons" describes an economic problem where individual users of a shared resource, acting in their own self-interest, collectively deplete that resource. Garrett Hardin popularized the concept using the analogy of a pasture open to all herders. ① Each herder has a rational incentive to add more cattle to their herd, as they receive the full benefit while the cost of overgrazing is shared by all. ② The cumulative effect of these individually rational decisions, however, is the degradation and destruction of the shared pasture. ③ This dynamic applies to many modern environmental problems like overfishing and air pollution. ④ This demonstrates that individual self-interest and the collective good are always perfectly aligned. ⑤ Solving the tragedy often requires some form of regulation, privatization, or community-based management to change the incentive structure.
