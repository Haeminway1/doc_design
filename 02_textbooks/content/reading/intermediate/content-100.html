The "is-ought problem," as articulated by philosopher David Hume, points to a fundamental gap between descriptive statements (what *is*) and prescriptive or normative statements (what *ought* to be). Hume observed that writers on morality often begin by making observations about human nature or the state of the world and then, without justification, suddenly shift to making claims about what is morally right or wrong. (A)____________, he argued that one cannot logically derive an "ought" from an "is." The fact that something is a certain way does not, in itself, imply that it should be that way. (B)____________, the fact that humans are naturally inclined towards self-interest does not logically entail that selfishness is morally good. This principle remains a central challenge in ethics, questioning the foundation of any moral system that claims to be derived from natural facts.

The "butterfly effect" is the most well-known concept from chaos theory, encapsulating the idea of sensitive dependence on initial conditions. The term, coined by meteorologist Edward Lorenz, metaphorically suggests that the flap of a butterfly's wings in Brazil could set off a tornado in Texas. This is not to suggest a direct, linear chain of causality. ________________________________________. In complex, non-linear systems like the weather, minuscule and immeasurable variations in the starting point can lead to vastly divergent outcomes over time. This makes long-term prediction not just difficult, but fundamentally impossible. The universe, according to chaos theory, is not the clockwork, predictable machine envisioned by Newtonian physics, but a system where deterministic laws can produce unpredictable and seemingly random behavior.
