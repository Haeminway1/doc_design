The "linguistic turn" of 20th-century philosophy was a profound shift that placed language at the center of philosophical inquiry. This movement was predicated on the idea that many traditional philosophical problems are actually pseudo-problems arising from a misunderstanding of language. The role of the philosopher, therefore, is not to solve these problems but to dissolve them by clarifying our use of words. Ludwig Wittgenstein, a key figure, argued that language functions like a "toolbox," with words having different uses in different "language-games." A philosophical confusion arises when we take a word from one language-game and try to apply it in another where it doesn't belong. For instance, treating the word "mind" as the name of a mysterious entity, rather than analyzing how we use the word in various psychological contexts, leads to intractable debates. Thus, philosophy becomes a therapeutic activity aimed at untangling these conceptual knots.

The "general will" (*volonté générale*), a central concept in Jean-Jacques Rousseau's political philosophy, is notoriously difficult to define. It is not simply the sum of all individual wills (the "will of all"), which may be based on private interests. Rather, the general will is the collective will of the citizen body, aimed exclusively at the common good. For a law to be legitimate, it must be an expression of this general will. This leads to Rousseau's most paradoxical formulation: that an individual who refuses to obey the general will can be "forced to be free." This is because, in Rousseau's view, the general will represents an individual's own true, rational will as a citizen, even if it conflicts with their immediate, selfish desires. True freedom, therefore, is not the absence of constraint but obedience to a law one has prescribed for oneself as part of the sovereign collective.
