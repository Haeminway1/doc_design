The concept of "pastiche," in postmodern art and literature, refers to the practice of imitating or combining the styles of previous artists and periods. Unlike parody, which mocks or satirizes the original style, pastiche is a neutral or even celebratory form of imitation. It is often described as "blank parody." In a world where genuine novelty and originality are seen as exhausted, the artist turns to the vast museum of past styles as a source of material. This results in works that are often eclectic, self-referential, and playful collages of historical forms. For critics like Fredric Jameson, pastiche is a key symptom of the postmodern condition. It reflects a culture that has lost its connection to history, resulting in a random and depthless cannibalization of past styles. The "precession of simulacra," a term from Jean Baudrillard, describes a four-stage historical evolution of the image, culminating in a postmodern state of hyperreality. In the first stage, the image is a faithful copy, a reflection of a profound reality. In the second, it masks and perverts that reality. In the third, it masks the *absence* of a profound reality. In the final stage, the image has no relation to any reality whatsoever; it is its own pure simulacrum. At this point, the simulation no longer imitates a real-world original.
