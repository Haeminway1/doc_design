The "Great Divergence" refers to the historical process by which the Western world, particularly Northwestern Europe, surged ahead of the rest of the world in terms of economic prosperity and technological capability during the 19th century. For centuries prior, major civilizations in Eurasia, such as China and the Islamic world, were on a comparable developmental footing with Europe. The question of why this divergence occurred is one of the most significant debates in global history. Some scholars, like David Landes, emphasize internal European factors such as a unique culture of science, capitalist institutions, and political fragmentation that fostered competition. Others, like Kenneth Pomeranz, argue for the crucial role of more contingent factors. They point to Europe's fortunate access to abundant coal and, most importantly, its exploitation of the Americas, which provided vast resources and a demographic outlet. This latter view challenges Eurocentric narratives by highlighting the global and often violent context of Europe's rise.

The sociological concept of "anomie," as developed by Émile Durkheim, describes a condition of societal breakdown where norms and values are weak or conflicting. This "normlessness" leaves individuals without moral guidance and a sense of purpose. Durkheim famously linked anomie to suicide rates, observing that they increased not only during economic downturns but also during periods of rapid prosperity. This latter finding is counterintuitive. His explanation is that in times of abrupt change, the traditional rules that regulate desire and aspiration lose their authority. Society's capacity to limit individuals' ambitions is diminished, leading to a state of perpetual, frustrating striving. This painful disjunction between limitless desires and the available means to satisfy them generates feelings of futility and despair. Thus, for Durkheim, a stable social order is crucial for integrating individuals and regulating their passions.
