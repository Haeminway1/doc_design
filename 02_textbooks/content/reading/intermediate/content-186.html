The "intentional stance," a concept from philosopher Daniel Dennett, is a strategy for interpreting an entity's behavior by treating it as if it were a rational agent with beliefs and desires. This is a pragmatic approach, not a metaphysical claim about the entity's inner consciousness. To predict a cat's behavior, for example, it is useful to attribute to it the "belief" that a mouse is behind the sofa and the "desire" to catch it. This is far more effective than trying to calculate the complex neurophysiological processes in the cat's brain. The stance works because evolution has designed systems that behave in ways that are well-predicted by this strategy. Therefore, the success of the intentional stance does not prove the existence of literal, human-like beliefs in the cat. It simply proves that the cat is a well-designed system whose behavior is efficiently captured by the logic of belief and desire.

The "backfire effect" is a cognitive bias that causes people to strengthen their beliefs when confronted with contradictory evidence. When a deeply held conviction is challenged by facts, it can be perceived as a threat to one's identity or worldview. This triggers a defensive, emotional response. Instead of impartially considering the new evidence, the individual may actively seek out flaws in the evidence and counter-arguments that support their original position. As a result, the attempt to correct misinformation can end up reinforcing it. For example, presenting a vaccine skeptic with data on vaccine safety may lead them to become even more entrenched in their anti-vaccine stance. This phenomenon is a major obstacle to reasoned debate and highlights the limitations of fact-based persuasion.
