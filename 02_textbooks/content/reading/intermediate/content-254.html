The "prisoner's dilemma" is a canonical example from game theory that shows why two completely rational individuals might not cooperate, even if it appears that it is in their best interests to do so. In the classic scenario, two accomplices are arrested and held in separate cells, unable to communicate. (A) The prosecutors offer each prisoner the same deal: if you betray the other (defect) and they remain silent, you go free and they get a long sentence. (B) If you both betray each other, you both get a moderate sentence. (C) If you both remain silent (cooperate), you both get a short sentence on a lesser charge. From each individual prisoner's perspective, betraying the other is always the dominant strategy, regardless of what the other does. If the other prisoner stays silent, betrayal is better than staying silent (freedom vs. short sentence). If the other prisoner betrays, betrayal is also better than staying silent (moderate sentence vs. long sentence). The dilemma arises because when both prisoners pursue their individual rational self-interest, they both end up with a worse outcome (a moderate sentence each) than if they had cooperated (a short sentence each).
