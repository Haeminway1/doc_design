The "Stockholm syndrome" is a psychological response sometimes seen in hostages or abuse victims, in which they develop positive feelings, such as sympathy or affection, toward their captors or abusers. This seemingly paradoxical reaction is considered a coping mechanism that develops under conditions of intense stress and danger. The syndrome is not a formal psychiatric diagnosis but a label for a pattern of behavior. The "affective fallacy" is a term from literary criticism, coined by W. K. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley, to describe the supposed error of evaluating a work of art by its emotional effect on the reader or audience. As key figures in the school of New Criticism, they argued that a poem or literary work is an autonomous, self-contained object whose meaning should be derived from its formal properties—its structure, imagery, meter, and wordplay—not from the psychological response it happens to produce. To judge a poem as "good" because it makes one feel sad, or "bad" because it is confusing, is to commit the affective fallacy. ________________________________________. This approach sought to make literary criticism a more objective and rigorous discipline, focused on the text itself rather than the subjective and variable impressions of its readers. While influential, this view has been challenged by later schools of thought, like reader-response criticism, which argue that the reader's role in creating meaning is essential.
