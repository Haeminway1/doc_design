Karl Marx's critique of political economy is centered on his analysis of the commodity and the nature of labor under capitalism. ( ① ) He argued that in a capitalist system, labor-power itself becomes a commodity, which the worker sells to the capitalist in exchange for a wage. ( ② ) The value of this commodity, like any other, is determined by the socially necessary labor time required to produce it—that is, the cost of keeping the worker alive and able to work. ( ③ ) The capitalist, however, can compel the worker to labor for a duration that exceeds this necessary time. ( ④ ) During this extra time, the worker produces value for which they are not compensated. ( ⑤ ) The entire system, therefore, is predicated on this extraction of unpaid labor, which is obscured by the wage form.

The educational theory of Lev Vygotsky emphasizes the social nature of learning and cognitive development. ( ① ) He argued that a child's development cannot be understood by looking at the child in isolation. ( ② ) Instead, learning is a fundamentally social process, mediated by language and interaction with more knowledgeable others. ( ③ ) Vygotsky made a key distinction between what a learner can achieve independently and what they can achieve with guidance and collaboration. ( ④ ) It represents the gap between a learner's actual developmental level and their potential developmental level.
