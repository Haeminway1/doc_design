The "problem of induction," as famously articulated by David Hume, questions the rational justification for believing that the future will resemble the past. We observe that the sun has risen every day in the past, and from this, we infer that it will rise again tomorrow. ① Hume pointed out that this inference is not based on deductive reason, as it is not a logical contradiction for the course of nature to change. ② Nor can it be justified by experience, as that would be a circular argument—using past experience to justify the validity of past experience. ③ Hume's conclusion was not that we should abandon inductive reasoning, but that its foundation is custom and habit, not reason. ④ This philosophical problem has been definitively solved by modern science through the use of statistical probability. ⑤ It remains a fundamental challenge to epistemology, forcing us to confront the limits of human knowledge and the foundations of the scientific method itself.

The "gothic" novel, a genre that flourished in the late 18th and early 19th centuries, is characterized by an atmosphere of mystery, horror, and dread. ① The setting is often a key element, typically an ancient, decaying castle or monastery with secret passages, dungeons, and a general sense of confinement. ② The plot frequently involves supernatural or inexplicable events, such as ghosts, curses, or monstrous transformations. ③ A central theme is the exploration of intense and often repressed emotions, challenging the Enlightenment's emphasis on reason and order. ④ The protagonists of gothic novels are almost always rational and emotionally stable individuals who easily overcome the challenges they face. ⑤ The genre delves into the darker aspects of the human psyche, exploring themes of transgression, madness, and the return of the repressed past.
