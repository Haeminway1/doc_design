In postcolonial theory, "hybridity" refers to the creation of new, transcultural forms within the contact zone produced by colonization. Homi K. Bhabha, a key theorist of hybridity, argues that colonial culture is never simply imposed on the colonized. Instead, the process is one of negotiation and translation, creating something new and ambivalent. For example, the adoption of the English language by colonized peoples often results in new dialects and literary forms that are "almost the same, but not quite" as the original. This "in-between" space, Bhabha contends, is a site of resistance. It disrupts the authority of colonial discourse by revealing that it is not a stable, universal entity, but is itself open to transformation. Hybridity thus challenges essentialist notions of cultural purity and authentic identity.

The "is-ought problem," first articulated by David Hume, highlights a fundamental logical gap in much of moral reasoning. Hume observed that writers often move from descriptive statements about what *is* the case to prescriptive statements about what *ought* to be the case, without providing any justification for this leap. He argued that one cannot validly derive an "ought" conclusion from premises that are purely "is" statements. The fact that something is a certain way in nature, for instance, does not logically entail that it is morally right or good. This principle is sometimes called "Hume's Guillotine" because it sharply severs facts from values. This challenge remains a central problem in meta-ethics, forcing any moral theory that seeks to ground itself in natural facts to explain how it bridges this logical chasm.
