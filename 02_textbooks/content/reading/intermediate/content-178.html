The "observer effect" in quantum mechanics is the principle that the act of measuring a phenomenon inevitably changes that phenomenon. This is not due to clumsy technique but is a fundamental aspect of the subatomic world. To observe an electron's position, for example, a physicist must interact with it, typically by bouncing a photon off it. This interaction necessarily imparts energy to the electron, altering its momentum. Therefore, one cannot know both the precise position and the precise momentum of a particle at the same time—a principle formalized in Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle. This reality fundamentally challenges the classical, Newtonian view of a detached observer who can view the world as it is, without influencing it. In the quantum realm, the observer and the observed are inextricably linked.

The "hermeneutics of suspicion" is a mode of interpretation, famously associated with Marx, Nietzsche, and Freud, that approaches texts with a fundamental skepticism. It operates on the assumption that the surface meaning of a text is a deception, masking a deeper, hidden truth. For Marx, this hidden truth was economic interest; for Nietzsche, the will to power; for Freud, repressed desire. The task of the critic, therefore, is to act as a demystifier, unmasking the hidden ideologies and motivations at play. This interpretive stance has been profoundly influential in modern critical theory. However, some contemporary theorists, like Rita Felski, have called for a "post-critical" approach. They argue that a relentless focus on suspicion can be reductive, overlooking other vital functions of literature, such as providing enchantment, recognition, and solace.
