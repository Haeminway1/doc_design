The "correspondence bias," also known as the fundamental attribution error, is the tendency to over-emphasize dispositional explanations for others' behaviors while under-emphasizing situational explanations. In other words, we are more likely to attribute someone's actions to their character ("that's the kind of person they are") rather than to the circumstances they are in. (A) For example, if we see a coworker snap at a client, we are more likely to think they are a rude person than to consider that they might be under immense personal stress. (B) This bias is not as strong when we explain our own negative behavior; in that case, we are more likely to blame the situation. (C) This cognitive shortcut, while often efficient, can lead to significant misunderstandings and unfair judgments in our social interactions.
