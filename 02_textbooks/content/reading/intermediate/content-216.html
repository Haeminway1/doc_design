The "agonistic" model of democracy, proposed by Chantal Mouffe, offers a critique of both consensus-based deliberative models and antagonistic models of politics. Mouffe argues that conflict and division are an ineradicable part of politics and that the goal of democracy is not to eliminate them. The aim of deliberative democracy—to reach a rational consensus free from passion—is an illusion. Instead, democratic politics should aim to transform "antagonism" (a struggle between enemies) into "agonism" (a struggle between adversaries). Adversaries are legitimate opponents who disagree on the interpretation of shared ethico-political principles. The "cyborg," as theorized by Donna Haraway, is a hybrid of organism and machine that serves as a powerful metaphor for challenging traditional dualisms. Haraway argues that in our technological world, the boundaries between human and animal, human and machine, and the physical and non-physical have become leaky and indistinct. The cyborg is a creature that confounds these neat categories. It is an ironic myth that subverts origin stories and the idea of a natural, unified self. For Haraway, this is a politically potent idea. By embracing our partial, artificial, and hybrid nature, we can build new political coalitions based on affinity and choice, rather than on supposedly "natural" identities like gender or race.
