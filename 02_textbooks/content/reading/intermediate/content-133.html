The "anthropic principle" in cosmology addresses the observation that the fundamental constants of the universe appear to be finely tuned to permit the existence of conscious life. ① The "weak" version of this principle is a relatively uncontroversial form of selection bias: we must observe conditions compatible with our existence because we would not be here to observe them otherwise. ② For example, we find ourselves on a planet with liquid water not because such planets are common, but because we could only have evolved on one. ③ The "strong" version, however, makes the more controversial claim that the universe must have had properties that allow life to develop at some stage in its history. ④ The multiverse hypothesis suggests that our universe is just one of many, each with different physical constants. ⑤ This stronger formulation is often criticized as being unscientific and teleological, as it seems to imply that the universe was in some way designed for us.

The "backfire effect" is a cognitive bias that can cause individuals who are confronted with evidence that contradicts their beliefs to reject the evidence and strengthen their support for their original stance. ① When a deeply held belief is challenged by facts, it can feel like a personal attack, triggering a defensive emotional reaction. ② Instead of updating their beliefs in light of the new information, people may double down, actively seeking out counterarguments and rationalizations to protect their worldview. ③ This phenomenon is a significant barrier to productive public discourse and personal learning, particularly on emotionally charged topics like politics or religion. ④ Cognitive dissonance is the mental discomfort experienced when holding two or more contradictory beliefs. ⑤ The backfire effect suggests that simply presenting people with facts is often an ineffective, and sometimes counterproductive, strategy for persuasion.
