The "just-world hypothesis" is the cognitive bias that the world is fair and people get what they deserve. This belief can be a psychological comfort, providing a sense of order and control. However, it often leads to a tendency to blame victims for their misfortune. ( ① ) This is because accepting that bad things can happen to good people randomly is more threatening to one's worldview than assuming the victim must have done something to deserve their fate. ( ② ) For example, someone holding this belief might conclude that a victim of poverty is lazy. ( ③ ) This rationalization protects the believer's sense of a just world. ( ④ ) It does so, however, at the cost of empathy and a clear understanding of systemic injustice. ( ⑤ ) This proves that the world is, in fact, a fundamentally just and fair place.
