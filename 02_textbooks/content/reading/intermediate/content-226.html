The "affective turn" in the humanities represents a significant shift in scholarly focus, moving away from a primary emphasis on language, discourse, and social construction towards an exploration of "affect." Unlike emotions, which are often understood as named, personal, and psychological states (like anger or happiness), affect is conceptualized as a pre-personal, visceral force or intensity that passes between bodies. It is the body's capacity to affect and be affected before that sensation is necessarily codified into a specific emotion. This perspective, championed by thinkers like Brian Massumi and Eve Sedgwick, does not seek to replace discursive analysis but to supplement it. It argues that by paying attention to these non-cognitive, non-linguistic forces, we can better understand phenomena ranging from the contagious atmosphere of a political rally to the gut feeling that drives financial markets. In essence, it reintroduces the body and its autonomic responses as a crucial site of social and political analysis.
