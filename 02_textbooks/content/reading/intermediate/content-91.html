The sociological concept of "emotional labor," as first defined by Arlie Hochschild, refers to the process of managing feelings and expressions to fulfill the emotional requirements of a job. This form of labor requires one to induce or suppress feeling in order to sustain an outward countenance that produces the proper state of mind in others. In the field of biosemiotics, the concept of "Umwelt," introduced by Jakob von Uexküll, is fundamental to understanding the subjective worlds of organisms. The Umwelt is the specific perceptual world in which an organism exists and acts; it is a "self-in-world" model. It is not the objective environment as described by physics, but rather a world composed of what is meaningful to that particular organism. For a tick, the Umwelt might consist only of a few key signs: the scent of butyric acid from a mammal's skin, the temperature of blood, and the texture of hair. ________________________________________. This radical focus on the organism's own point of view challenges an anthropocentric perspective that assumes a single, objective reality accessible to all. It reveals a vast multiplicity of subjective worlds, each tailored to the specific needs and capacities of a given form of life.
