The "Dunning-Kruger effect" is a cognitive bias whereby people with low ability at a task overestimate their competence. This is not a matter of ego, but rather a metacognitive deficit: the skills needed to produce a correct response are often the very same skills needed to recognize a correct response. ( ① ) Thus, the incompetent suffer a dual burden—not only do they make mistakes, but their incompetence robs them of the ability to realize it. ( ② ) Conversely, highly skilled individuals may underestimate their relative competence, erroneously assuming that tasks easy for them are also easy for others. ( ③ ) This effect has significant implications for education and self-improvement. ( ④ ) It suggests that for low-performers to recognize their incompetence, they must first gain a degree of competence. ( ⑤ ) This is why constructive feedback and guided training are crucial; they not only improve skill but also enhance the metacognitive ability for accurate self-assessment.
