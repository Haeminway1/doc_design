The "categorical imperative," as formulated by Immanuel Kant, is the foundational principle of his deontological ethics. It is a command of reason that is absolute and unconditional, binding on all rational beings. Kant's first formulation states: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." This means that the morality of an action is not judged by its consequences, but by whether its guiding principle can be consistently applied to everyone without contradiction. For example, a maxim of lying to get out of trouble cannot be universalized, because if everyone lied, trust would be destroyed and lying would no longer be an effective means to any end. Thus, the test of morality is one of rational consistency, not of utility or happiness.

The "Turing test" is a test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from that of a human. In the test, a human evaluator holds a text-based conversation with a human and a machine, without knowing which is which. If the evaluator cannot reliably tell them apart, the machine is said to have passed. Alan Turing, who proposed the test, intended it as a pragmatic replacement for the philosophically fraught question, "Can machines think?" It is a purely behavioral test. It does not and cannot test for genuine consciousness, self-awareness, or subjective experience. A machine could, in theory, pass the test through clever programming and vast databases without any inner understanding. Therefore, passing the test is a significant milestone in artificial intelligence, but it is not proof of sentience.
