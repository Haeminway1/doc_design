The "panopticon," as conceived by Jeremy Bentham, is an architectural design for a prison that has become a powerful metaphor for modern surveillance. It consists of a central tower from which a single guard can observe all the inmates in a surrounding ring of cells. ① The inmates, however, cannot see the guard, and thus never know if they are being watched at any given moment. ② This uncertainty is the key to its power; it forces the inmates to regulate their own behavior as if they are under constant surveillance. ③ The panopticon, therefore, is a system of control that operates through direct and constant physical coercion by the guards. ④ Michel Foucault famously used this concept to argue that modern society itself functions like a panopticon, with disciplinary power operating subtly through schools, hospitals, and workplaces. ⑤ The individual internalizes the gaze of power, becoming their own overseer.

The "availability heuristic" is a mental shortcut where people judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind. Events that are recent, vivid, or emotionally charged are more "available" in our memory and thus seem more probable than they actually are.
