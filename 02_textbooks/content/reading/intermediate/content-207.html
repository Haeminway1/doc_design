The "event," in the philosophy of Alain Badiou, is a rare and unpredictable occurrence that ruptures the existing state of a situation and opens up the possibility of a new truth. An event is not simply a fact or an incident within the established order. It is something that, from the perspective of the existing situation and its knowledge, is impossible or unthinkable. The French Revolution, the invention of a new artistic form, or a transformative romantic encounter can all be considered events. An event does not carry its own truth automatically. A truth is produced only when individuals, or "subjects," declare their fidelity to the event. This means they commit to thinking and acting in accordance with the new possibility that the event has introduced into the world. The concept of "biopolitics," as Foucault outlines it, marks a fundamental shift in the nature of state power. In the age of sovereignty, power was exercised primarily as a right of deduction, a right to "take life or let live." The sovereign's power was most clearly demonstrated in his ability to kill. Modern power, however, operates differently. It is a "power over life," one that seeks to administer, manage, and foster the life of the population. This "biopower" is concerned with phenomena like birth rates, public health, longevity, and sanitation. It is a productive power that aims to optimize the vitality of the collective social body. The state becomes a manager of life, seeking to ensure a healthy, productive, and orderly populace.
