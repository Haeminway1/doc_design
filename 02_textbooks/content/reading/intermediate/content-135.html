The "just-world hypothesis" is a cognitive bias and belief that the world is fundamentally just, leading people to assume that actions will have morally fair and fitting consequences. ① This belief implies that noble actions will eventually be rewarded and evil actions will eventually be punished. ② A strong belief in a just world can be a coping mechanism, providing a sense of order, predictability, and control in a sometimes chaotic world. ③ However, this bias can also have negative social consequences, such as a tendency to blame victims for their misfortune. ④ For example, someone holding this belief might assume that a victim of poverty or assault must have done something to deserve their fate, as this is more comforting than accepting that terrible things can happen to good people randomly. ⑤ This rationalization serves to protect the individual's belief in a just world, but at the cost of empathy and a clear understanding of systemic injustice.

The "Pollyanna principle," or positivity bias, is the psychological tendency for people to remember pleasant items more accurately than unpleasant ones. ① The term originates from a novel about a young girl who finds something to be glad about in every situation. ② Research has shown that on a subconscious level, the mind tends to focus on the optimistic, and people tend to use positive words more frequently than negative ones. ③ This bias is not to say that people ignore all negative information, but that there is a general predisposition toward the positive. ④ The negativity bias, in contrast, is the psychological phenomenon by which humans have a greater recall of unpleasant memories compared with positive memories. ⑤ This positivity bias may have an evolutionary basis, as an optimistic outlook can foster resilience, motivation, and social bonding, which are crucial for survival and well-being.
