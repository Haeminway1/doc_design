The "disenchantment of the world" (*Entzauberung der Welt*) is a concept introduced by sociologist Max Weber to describe a key characteristic of modernity. It refers to the process by which the world is stripped of its magical, mystical, and spiritual significance through the rise of rationalism, science, and bureaucracy. In pre-modern societies, the world was an "enchanted garden," full of spirits, mysterious forces, and divine interventions. Modernity, however, replaces this worldview with the belief that all things can, in principle, be mastered by calculation and technical control. There are no longer any mysterious, incalculable forces at play. This process of rationalization leads to immense technological and material progress, but it comes at a cost. For Weber, the disenchanted world is one that is also devoid of ultimate meaning and value, leading to a sense of alienation and confinement within the "iron cage" of bureaucracy. The "carnivalesque," as theorized by Mikhail Bakhtin, describes a literary and cultural mode in which dominant social hierarchies, norms, and prohibitions are temporarily suspended and subverted through humor, chaos, and grotesque realism. Bakhtin studied the medieval carnival as a social institution where this spirit was most evident. During carnival, all were considered equal, and the sacred was profaned, the high was brought low, and the body was celebrated in its most earthy and grotesque aspects—eating, drinking, and procreation. This was not a purely negative, destructive force. Rather, it was a source of regeneration and renewal, a temporary return to a utopian state of freedom and abundance.
