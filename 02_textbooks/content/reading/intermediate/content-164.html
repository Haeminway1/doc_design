The "veil of ignorance" is a thought experiment proposed by John Rawls to determine the principles of a just society. He asks us to imagine ourselves in an "original position," where we must choose these principles without knowing our own place in society. ① Behind this veil, we are ignorant of our race, gender, wealth, talents, and even our conception of the good life. ② This ignorance prevents us from designing principles that are biased in our own favor. ③ Rawls argues that from this impartial standpoint, rational individuals would choose principles that guarantee equal basic liberties and arrange inequalities to be of the greatest benefit to the least advantaged. ④ This thought experiment is intended to be a literal description of a historical event that actually occurred. ⑤ The purpose of the veil is to model a fair and impartial procedure for arriving at the principles of justice.

The "Sapir-Whorf hypothesis" posits a relationship between the language one speaks and how one perceives and conceptualizes the world. The "strong" version, linguistic determinism, claims that language dictates thought. ① This strong form is now largely discredited. ② The "weak" version, linguistic relativity, is more widely accepted. ③ It suggests that language influences, rather than determines, our thought patterns and perceptions. ④ For example, a language that has multiple words for different types of snow may encourage its speakers to pay more attention to and perceive finer distinctions in snow. ⑤ This proves that all human beings, regardless of their language, perceive the world in an identical way.

  • (2) ②
