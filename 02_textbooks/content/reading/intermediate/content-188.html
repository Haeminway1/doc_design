The "rhizome" is a philosophical concept from Deleuze and Guattari that models a non-hierarchical, interconnected way of thinking. It is contrasted with the "arborescent" or tree model, which has a central root, a trunk, and a linear, branching structure. The rhizome, like an underground network of grass roots, has no center and no pre-established path. Any point can be connected to any other point. This model encourages "nomadic thought," which moves across different fields and makes unforeseen connections. For example, one could connect a concept from music theory to an idea in urban planning without following a traditional disciplinary hierarchy. This is in stark contrast to arborescent thought, which would demand a logical, step-by-step progression from a foundational principle.

The "panopticon," an architectural design for a prison by Jeremy Bentham, functions as a powerful metaphor for modern disciplinary power. The design involves a central tower from which a guard can see into all the surrounding cells, but the inmates in the cells cannot see the guard. This creates a state of conscious and permanent visibility for the inmates. The key to its power is this uncertainty; because the inmate never knows when they are being watched, they must behave as if they are always being watched. This internalizes the disciplinary gaze, making the inmate their own overseer. Michel Foucault extended this concept, arguing that modern society itself operates like a panopticon. He contended that power in schools, hospitals, and factories functions not through overt force, but through this subtle, pervasive surveillance that encourages self-regulation.
