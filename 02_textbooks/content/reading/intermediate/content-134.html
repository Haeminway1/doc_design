The "contact hypothesis" in social psychology posits that prejudice between groups can be reduced by direct contact, but only under certain optimal conditions. ① First proposed by Gordon Allport, the theory specifies that for contact to be effective, it must involve equal status between the groups in the situation. ② Second, the groups must work together towards common goals, fostering intergroup cooperation. ③ Third, there must be institutional support for the contact from authorities, laws, or customs. ④ The implicit-association test (IAT) is a controversial psychological test designed to measure unconscious biases. ⑤ Finally, the contact should be of a personal and informal nature, allowing individuals to get to know each other as individuals rather than as stereotypes. When these conditions are met, contact is a powerful tool for fostering intergroup understanding.

The "Baader-Meinhof phenomenon," or frequency illusion, is a cognitive bias where, after noticing something for the first time, one has a tendency to notice it more often, leading to the belief that it has an increased frequency of occurrence. ① This illusion is the result of two psychological processes working in tandem. ② The first is selective attention: once your brain is primed to a new concept, it is more likely to pick it out from your environment. ③ The second is confirmation bias: each new sighting is interpreted as further proof of the object's sudden ubiquity, confirming your belief that its frequency has genuinely increased. ④ Synesthesia is a neurological condition in which stimulation of one sensory pathway leads to automatic, involuntary experiences in a second sensory pathway. ⑤ In reality, the frequency of the thing you noticed has not changed at all; only your perception of it has, creating a subjective and often amusing illusion.
