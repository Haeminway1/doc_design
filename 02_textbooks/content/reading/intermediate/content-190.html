The "affect heuristic" is a mental shortcut that involves making decisions based on one's current emotional state. If we feel good about an activity, we tend to judge its risks as low and its benefits as high. Conversely, if we feel bad about it, we judge its risks as high and its benefits as low. This reliance on "gut feelings" allows for quick and efficient decision-making, which can be advantageous in many situations. However, it can also lead to systematic errors in judgment. For instance, a vivid media report about a rare disease can evoke fear, leading people to overestimate their personal risk, while the more abstract, statistical dangers of common activities like driving are underestimated. This demonstrates how our emotional responses can systematically diverge from a rational assessment of probability and utility.

The "bystander effect" is a social psychological theory that states individuals are less likely to offer help to a victim when there are other people present. The first laboratory experiments exploring this phenomenon were conducted by John Darley and Bibb Latané. They were spurred by the infamous 1964 murder of Kitty Genovese, where numerous neighbors reportedly heard her screams but failed to intervene. Darley and Latané identified two key psychological mechanisms responsible for this effect. The first is "diffusion of responsibility," where the presence of others makes each individual feel less personally responsible for acting. The second is "pluralistic ignorance," where bystanders look to each other for cues, and if no one is reacting, they interpret the situation as a non-emergency.
