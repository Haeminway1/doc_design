Standpoint theory, a key concept in feminist epistemology, challenges the traditional notion of a neutral, objective, and universal "view from nowhere." ( ① ) It argues that knowledge is always situated and that the social position of the knower significantly shapes what can be known. ( ② ) Specifically, it posits that marginalized groups, because of their position within power structures, have the potential to achieve a more complete and less distorted understanding of society than dominant groups. ( ③ ) The perspective of the powerful tends to take the existing social order for granted and obscure the realities of oppression. ( ④ ) In contrast, the oppressed must understand both their own world and the world of the dominant in order to survive, giving them a "double vision." ( ⑤ ) By critically reflecting on their shared experiences of marginalization, they can achieve a standpoint that reveals the hidden workings of power.

The relationship between technology and society is a central theme in media studies, famously encapsulated by Marshall McLuhan's aphorism, "the medium is the message." ( ① ) This statement suggests that the most significant effect of a medium is not the content it carries, but the way the medium itself changes our patterns of perception and social organization. ( ② ) For example, the content of a television program is less important than the way the medium of television itself fosters a passive, visual, and non-linear mode of experiencing the world. ( ③ ) The invention of the printing press, in this view, did not just spread new ideas; it fostered individualism, nationalism, and linear, logical thinking. ( ④ ) McLuhan's work is often seen as a prime example of a particular theoretical stance. ( ⑤ ) This perspective has been criticized for being overly simplistic and for downplaying the role of human agency and social context in shaping how technology is used.
