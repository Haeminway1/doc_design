The "paradox of thrift," a concept central to Keynesian economics, posits that while saving is beneficial for an individual, a collective attempt by everyone to save more can be detrimental to the overall economy. ( ① ) If a large portion of the population simultaneously increases their savings, the total demand for goods and services will fall. ( ② ) In response to this drop in aggregate demand, businesses will cut production and lay off workers. ( ③ ) This, in turn, leads to a decrease in overall national income. ( ④ ) This illustrates a "fallacy of composition"—the error of assuming that what is true for a part is necessarily true for the whole. ( ⑤ ) As a result, the total amount of saving in the economy may actually decrease, because people have less income from which to save, even though each individual intended to save more.
