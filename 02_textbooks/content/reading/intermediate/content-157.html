The "tessellation" of a flat surface is the tiling of a plane using one or more geometric shapes, called tiles, with no overlaps and no gaps. In mathematics, tessellations can be regular, using only one type of regular polygon, or semi-regular, using a variety of regular polygons. ① Only three regular polygons can tile the plane by themselves: the equilateral triangle, the square, and the regular hexagon. ② This is because the internal angle of the polygon must be a divisor of 360 degrees to ensure the vertices can meet at a point without leaving a gap. ③ The artist M. C. Escher was famous for his use of tessellations, often with irregular, interlocking figures of animals and other forms. ④ The study of tessellations is a purely abstract mathematical exercise with no practical applications in the real world. ⑤ From the honeycomb structure created by bees to the patterns in crystal structures and the design of floor tiles, tessellations are a fundamental concept in both nature and human design.

The "categorical imperative" is the central concept in the moral philosophy of Immanuel Kant. It is a command of reason that is unconditional and binding on all rational agents, regardless of their personal desires. (A) Kant's first formulation of this imperative is: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." (B) This provides a formal test for the morality of an action: if a maxim cannot be universalized without contradiction, it is morally forbidden. (C) For example, a maxim of making false promises cannot be universalized because it would destroy the very institution of promising. (D) Therefore, the morality of an action is determined by its consequences, specifically whether it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number. (E) For Kant, this grounds morality in the universal structure of reason itself, rather than in subjective feelings or external authorities.
