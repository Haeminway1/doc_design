The "affective turn" in the humanities and social sciences represents a significant shift away from models of inquiry centered on language, discourse, and representation. ① It challenges the post-structuralist emphasis on the text and instead focuses on "affect"—the visceral, pre-personal, and non-conscious forces that pass between bodies. ② Affects are intensities, not yet codified into named emotions like "anger" or "joy," that shape our capacity to act and be acted upon. ③ This perspective does not discard the analysis of language but rather seeks to understand how language and affect are intertwined. ④ The primary goal of affect theory is to create a more precise and objective system for categorizing human emotions. ⑤ By attending to these non-linguistic forces, scholars can analyze phenomena like the contagious atmosphere of a crowd or the gut feeling of a financial market, which are difficult to grasp through purely discursive analysis.
