The "flâneur," a key figure in the writings of Charles Baudelaire and Walter Benjamin, is the quintessential urban observer of 19th-century Paris. The flâneur is a man of the crowd, an idler and a stroller who wanders the city streets to experience and observe the spectacle of modern life. He is detached yet deeply immersed, an anonymous spectator in the bustling arcades and boulevards. His purpose is not to get from one place to another, but to see and be seen, to read the city as a text. The flâneur is a product of a specific historical moment: the rise of the modern metropolis and consumer capitalism. For Benjamin, the flâneur represents a form of resistance to the disciplined, goal-oriented pace of modern industrial life. The "subaltern," a term adopted by postcolonial theorist Gayatri Spivak, refers to those groups in society who are subject to the hegemony of the ruling class and are excluded from the dominant channels of representation. These are people whose voices are not heard in the historical record, whose experiences are not recognized as valid. In her famous essay, Spivak asks, "Can the Subaltern Speak?" She argues that the answer is no. This is not because the subaltern is literally mute, but because any attempt to speak is filtered through the dominant discourse. When the subaltern tries to speak for herself, her speech is either not heard or is reinterpreted to fit the categories of the powerful. Even the well-intentioned intellectual who tries to "speak for" the subaltern inevitably participates in this silencing by imposing their own framework.
