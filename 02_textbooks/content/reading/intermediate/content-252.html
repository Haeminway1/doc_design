The concept of "hauntology," a term coined by Jacques Derrida, describes the persistent and unsettling presence of the past within the present, particularly the way in which lost or unrealized futures continue to shape contemporary experience. Playing on the French words for "haunting" (*hantise*) and "ontology" (*ontologie*), the term suggests a mode of being that is defined by what is no longer or not yet. Derrida initially used it to discuss the spectral legacy of Marxism after the "end of history." The promise of a communist future, though seemingly defeated, continues to "haunt" the present, revealing the inadequacies of triumphant liberal capitalism. Cultural critic Mark Fisher later expanded this idea to diagnose a broader cultural condition in the 21st century, which he saw as characterized by a pervasive nostalgia and an inability to imagine a future that is radically different from the past. For Fisher, our culture is haunted by the ghosts of lost futures.
