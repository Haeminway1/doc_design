The "sociological imagination," a term coined by C. Wright Mills, is the ability to see the connection between personal troubles and public issues. It is a quality of mind that allows one to grasp the interplay of individual biography and historical social structures. A person who is unemployed, for example, might view their situation as a personal failure. The sociological imagination, however, allows them to see their personal trouble in the context of larger public issues, such as economic recession, deindustrialization, or systemic inequality. It is the capacity to shift from one perspective to another—from the political to the psychological; from the examination of a single family to the comparative assessment of the national budgets of the world. The "symbolic," in Lacanian psychoanalysis, is one of the three orders that structure human existence, alongside the Imaginary and the Real. The Symbolic order is the realm of language, law, social norms, and cultural structures. It is the world of the "big Other," the network of signifiers into which a human subject is born. Entry into the Symbolic marks a crucial moment of alienation. The child must renounce the pre-linguistic, dyadic unity with the mother (the Imaginary) and accept the "Law-of-the-Father," which is the set of rules and prohibitions that govern society. This entry into language means that our desires and our very sense of self are no longer immediate but are mediated through a system of signifiers that we do not control. We can never say exactly what we mean, as our experience is always filtered through the pre-existing structure of language.
