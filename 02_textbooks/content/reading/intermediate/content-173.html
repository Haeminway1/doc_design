The "Pygmalion effect," or Rosenthal effect, is a powerful psychological phenomenon demonstrating the force of self-fulfilling prophecies. It posits that higher expectations placed upon an individual lead to an increase in their performance. A classic study in this area involved researchers informing public school teachers that certain randomly selected students were "intellectual bloomers" poised for significant growth. The researchers then observed the interactions and measured the outcomes at the end of the school year. The students who had been labeled as bloomers showed significantly greater improvement in their IQ scores than their peers. The study concluded that the teachers' expectations, though based on false information, created a warmer climate and provided more challenging material, thereby facilitating the students' success. This effect underscores the profound impact that interpersonal expectations can have on achievement.

The "affective fallacy," a term coined by New Critics W. K. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley, warns against a specific error in literary interpretation. It is the fallacy of evaluating a literary work based on the emotional response it elicits in the reader. For these critics, a poem is an autonomous, self-contained object whose meaning and value reside in its formal properties, such as structure, imagery, and irony. To judge a work as "good" because it makes one feel happy, or "bad" because it is confusing, is to shift the focus from the text to the reader's subjective and variable impressions. This approach, they argued, leads to a chaotic and impressionistic relativism, undermining the possibility of objective literary analysis. Therefore, the New Critics advocated for a "close reading" method that focused exclusively on the text itself. This perspective sought to establish literary criticism as a more rigorous and objective discipline.
