The "culture industry," a term coined by Frankfurt School theorists Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer, refers to the mass production of standardized cultural goods under capitalism. They argued that popular culture—films, music, magazines—is not an authentic expression of the masses but a top-down industry designed to ensure profitability and social conformity. The apparent variety of cultural products is illusory. Beneath the surface, these products follow predictable formulas and promote the values of the dominant ideology. The culture industry pacifies its audience with easy, repetitive pleasures, stifling the capacity for critical thought and genuine individuality. This process, they contended, creates a "one-dimensional" society where dissent is neutralized and the status quo is endlessly reproduced. The ultimate effect is a subtle but powerful form of social control.

The "correspondence bias," or fundamental attribution error, is a pervasive tendency in social cognition to overestimate the role of dispositional factors and underestimate situational factors when explaining others' behavior. In other words, we are quick to assume that a person's actions reflect their character rather than the circumstances they are in. For example, if a driver cuts us off in traffic, our immediate reaction is often to think they are a selfish or reckless person. We are less likely to consider situational possibilities, such as that they might be rushing to a hospital for an emergency. This bias is not as strong when we explain our own behavior, a phenomenon known as the actor-observer asymmetry. This cognitive shortcut, while often efficient, can lead to unfair judgments, interpersonal conflict, and a failure to appreciate the power of social context.
