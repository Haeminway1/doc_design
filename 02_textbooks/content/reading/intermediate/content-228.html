The "Stockholm syndrome" is a psychological phenomenon in which hostages express empathy and sympathy and have positive feelings toward their captors, sometimes to the point of defending them. This seemingly paradoxical response is considered a coping mechanism developed under the duress of a terrifying and life-threatening situation. ( ① ) Several factors are thought to contribute to its development, including the intensity of the experience, the duration of the captivity, and the victim's dependence on the captor for survival. ( ② ) The victim, isolated from other perspectives and experiencing small acts of kindness from the captor, may begin to see the situation from the aggressor's point of view. ( ③ ) This is not a formal psychiatric diagnosis but a label for a pattern of behaviors. ( ④ ) Understanding this phenomenon is crucial for law enforcement negotiators and mental health professionals who work with victims of kidnapping and abuse. ( ⑤ ) This conscious and deliberate strategy is employed by victims to manipulate their captors.
