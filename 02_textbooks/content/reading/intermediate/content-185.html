The "Pollyanna principle," or positivity bias, is the psychological tendency to focus on and remember positive information more than negative information. This bias manifests in various ways, from the words we use to the memories we recall. For example, studies of language consistently find a universal tendency for positive words to be more frequent and varied than negative words. In memory recall tasks, people tend to remember more pleasant events from their past than unpleasant ones. This is not to say that people are naively optimistic or ignore all threats. Rather, it suggests a subconscious cognitive mechanism that leans toward the positive, which may have evolutionary benefits for resilience and motivation. This principle highlights a fundamental asymmetry in how the human mind processes emotional information.

The "fallacy of the single cause" is a logical error that occurs when it is assumed that there is one, simple cause for an outcome when in reality it may have been caused by a number of jointly sufficient causes. This oversimplification is common in complex debates. For instance, in analyzing a major historical event like a revolution, one might attribute it solely to the actions of a single leader or to a single economic crisis. This ignores the complex interplay of social, political, economic, and cultural factors that likely contributed to the event. A more robust analysis would recognize that the outcome was the result of a confluence of multiple factors. This fallacy is a tempting shortcut, but it ultimately hinders a deep and accurate understanding of complex phenomena.
