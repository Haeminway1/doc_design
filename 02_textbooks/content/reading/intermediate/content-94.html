The "intentional fallacy," a concept central to the school of New Criticism, refers to the error of judging a literary work by assuming the intent or purpose of its author. W. K. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley argued that the author's intention is ultimately unavailable and irrelevant to the interpretation of the text. The "free rider problem" is a classic challenge in economics and political science that arises in the context of public goods. Public goods are defined by two key characteristics: they are non-excludable (it is difficult or impossible to prevent anyone from using them) and non-rivalrous (one person's use does not diminish another's). National defense and clean air are classic examples. Because people can benefit from these goods without paying for them, they have an incentive to "free ride" on the contributions of others. ________________________________________. This can lead to the under-provision or even the complete absence of essential public goods, as everyone waits for someone else to bear the cost. Solving this problem often requires some form of coercion, such as taxation, or the creation of social norms that encourage contribution.
