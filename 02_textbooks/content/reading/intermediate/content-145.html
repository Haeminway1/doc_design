The Hegelian dialectic, a cornerstone of German Idealism, posits a process of development in which a concept or 'thesis' inevitably generates its opposite, the 'antithesis'. ① The conflict between these two is not a zero-sum game but is resolved through a higher-level synthesis, or *Aufhebung*. ② This synthesis preserves the rational elements of both the thesis and antithesis while transcending their limitations. ③ The process is not linear but cyclical, with each synthesis becoming a new thesis for a subsequent dialectical movement. ④ This new thesis, however, represents a complete negation of the previous stage, discarding all its elements to start anew. ⑤ It is through this perpetual motion of negation and sublation that Spirit, or Absolute Idea, comes to know itself in a progressively more comprehensive manner. Thus, for Hegel, history and thought unfold through this dynamic, triadic structure.
