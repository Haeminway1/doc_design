The "repressive hypothesis," which Michel Foucault critiques in *The History of Sexuality*, is the common belief that sexuality was severely repressed during the Victorian era and only liberated in the 20th century. Foucault argues that this view is misleading. He contends that the 19th century was not an age of silence about sex, but an era that witnessed an explosion of discourse about it. Sexuality became an object of intense scientific, medical, and psychiatric scrutiny. Doctors, priests, and educators produced a vast body of knowledge about sexual perversions, childhood sexuality, and marital duties. This was not a project of repression, but a new form of power.   • (3) manage, classify, and regulate it in minute detail
  • (4) return to a pre-modern state of blissful ignorance
  • (5) eliminate all forms of discourse related to sex

The "anthropic principle" in cosmology notes that the fundamental constants of the universe (such as the strength of gravity or the mass of an electron) appear to be finely tuned to allow for the existence of life. If these values were even slightly different, stars, planets, and conscious observers would not have been able to form. The "weak" version of this principle states that this is a selection bias: we can only observe a universe that is compatible with our existence. The "strong" version, however, makes a more radical claim: that the universe *must* have had properties that allow life to develop at some stage. This has led some to speculate about a "multiverse." If there are countless universes, each with different physical constants, it is not surprising that we find ourselves in one of the rare ones where the conditions are just right.   • (4) was deliberately created for the purpose of human life
  • (5) has physical laws that are constantly changing
