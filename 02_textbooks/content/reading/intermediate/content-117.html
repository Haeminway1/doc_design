The Frankfurt School's seminal work, *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, offers a profound and pessimistic critique of modernity. ( ① ) Adorno and Horkheimer posited that the Enlightenment, which promised to liberate humanity from myth and superstition through reason, had paradoxically turned into its opposite. ( ② ) This form of reason, focused solely on efficiency and self-preservation, treats both external nature and internal human nature as things to be dominated. ( ③ ) The culture industry, for example, exemplifies this process by producing standardized cultural goods that manipulate the masses into passive consumption, thereby reinforcing social control. ( ④ ) The ultimate outcome is a totally administered society where individuals believe they are free but are in fact enmeshed in a seamless web of coercion. ( ⑤ ) Thus, the mythic fate that the Enlightenment sought to escape returns in the form of a new, rationalized system of domination.

In his influential work on postcolonial theory, Homi K. Bhabha challenges simplistic binary oppositions between colonizer and colonized. ( ① ) He argues against the notion that colonial power operates through a one-way imposition of culture onto a passive native population. ( ② ) Instead, he focuses on the complex and often contradictory dynamics of the colonial encounter. ( ③ ) Bhabha introduces the concept of "mimicry," where the colonized subject imitates the colonizer's language, behavior, and culture. ( ④ ) This imitation, however, is never a perfect copy; it is "almost the same, but not quite." ( ⑤ ) It is in this gap, this slight difference, that the supposedly stable and universal authority of colonial discourse begins to fracture, revealing its own contingency and lack of a fixed origin.
