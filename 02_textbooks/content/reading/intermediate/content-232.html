The "trolley problem" is a famous thought experiment in ethics that highlights the tension between two major moral frameworks: utilitarianism and deontology. In the basic scenario, a runaway trolley is about to kill five people tied to the main track. You can pull a lever, which will divert the trolley to a side track where it will kill one person. A utilitarian, aiming to produce the greatest good for the greatest number, would likely argue for pulling the lever, as saving five lives at the cost of one is the better outcome. A deontologist, however, focuses on moral duties and rules. They might argue that pulling the lever is an intentional act of killing, which is inherently wrong regardless of the consequences. The problem becomes more complex in variations, such as a scenario where you must physically push a large person onto the track to stop the trolley. Many who would pull the lever in the first case would not push the person in the second, revealing the complex and often contradictory nature of our moral intuitions.
