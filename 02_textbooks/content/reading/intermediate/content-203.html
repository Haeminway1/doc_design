The "gaze," as theorized by feminist film critic Laura Mulvey, is a concept that analyzes how visual media are structured around a masculine perspective. In her seminal essay, "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema," Mulvey argued that in classical Hollywood films, the camera's perspective, and by extension the audience's, aligns with that of a male protagonist. Women are typically presented as passive objects of this "male gaze," their bodies fragmented and fetishized for the erotic pleasure of the male viewer. This is not a neutral act of looking; it is an ideological operation that reinforces patriarchal power by naturalizing the objectification of women. The on-screen woman is defined by her "to-be-looked-at-ness," her presence often halting the narrative to invite scopophilic contemplation. The "chronotope," a concept developed by literary theorist Mikhail Bakhtin, refers to the intrinsic connectedness of temporal and spatial relationships as they are artistically expressed in literature. The term literally means "time-space." Bakhtin argued that in a literary work, time and space are not empty containers but are fused into a concrete, meaningful whole. Different genres are characterized by different chronotopes. For example, the "adventure-time" of the Greek romance features a vast, abstract geography where heroes undergo trials over a long but essentially empty and uneventful stretch of time. In contrast, the chronotope of the "road" in a picaresque novel fuses the journey through space with the unfolding of a life's narrative. For Bakhtin, the chronotope is the primary organizing center for the narrative's events, determining how characters are portrayed and how the plot unfolds.
