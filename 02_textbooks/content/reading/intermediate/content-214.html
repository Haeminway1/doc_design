The concept of "performativity," as developed by J. L. Austin and later adapted by Judith Butler, challenges the traditional view of language as a purely descriptive tool. Austin distinguished between "constative" utterances, which describe a state of affairs (e.g., "The cat is on the mat"), and "performative" utterances, which *do* something in the act of being said. For example, saying "I promise" or "I apologize" is not describing an action; it *is* the action of promising or apologizing. These utterances bring into being the state of affairs they refer to. Judith Butler extended this concept to gender, arguing that gender is not a descriptive attribute but a performative accomplishment.   • (2) our words and actions can actively construct the social reality they seem to describe
  • (3) the distinction between saying and doing is always clear and absolute
  • (4) gender is a fixed, biological essence that is independent of our actions
  • (5) all linguistic utterances are purely descriptive in nature

The "dramaturgical analysis" of Erving Goffman views social interaction as a theatrical performance. People are actors on a stage, managing their "impression" to present a particular version of themselves to an audience. This involves a careful manipulation of setting, appearance, and manner. A key element of this model is the distinction between the "front stage" and the "backstage." The front stage is where the performance is given, where the actor adheres to the expected social script. The backstage is a private region where the actor can drop the performance, relax, and prepare for future interactions. For example, a server in a restaurant is on the front stage when interacting with customers, but in the kitchen, they enter the backstage. This model suggests that ________________________________________.
Which of the following best completes the blank?
  • (1) there is no difference between how people behave in public and in private
  • (2) all human behavior is spontaneous and completely unscripted
  • (3) the "self" is not a fixed entity but a fluid performance that varies by context
  • (4) social interaction is primarily governed by economic motives
  • (5) the backstage is where individuals present their most idealized and perfect self
