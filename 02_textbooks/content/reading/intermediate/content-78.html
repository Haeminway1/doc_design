The "Overton window" is a model in political science that describes the range of ideas the public is willing to consider and accept as legitimate for political discourse. This "window" of acceptable policies can shift over time, moving an idea from being unthinkable, to radical, to acceptable, to sensible, and finally to popular policy. The theory posits that politicians are generally limited to supporting policies that fall within the Overton window if they wish to remain in office. The model provides a framework for understanding how public opinion shapes and constrains the possibilities of political action.

In the philosophy of mind, "eliminative materialism" is a radical form of physicalism that argues that our common-sense understanding of the mind is fundamentally flawed and that certain classes of mental states that we talk about do not actually exist. Proponents like Paul and Patricia Churchland contend that our everyday psychological vocabulary—concepts like "belief," "desire," "fear," and "sensation"—is part of a primitive, pre-scientific theory they call "folk psychology." They argue that as neuroscience advances, this folk psychology will be shown to be so radically inaccurate that it will not be reduced to neuroscientific concepts, but will instead be eliminated entirely. ________________________________________. This would be analogous to how modern science eliminated concepts like "phlogiston" or "the celestial spheres" rather than reducing them to more fundamental theories. The view is highly controversial, as it asks us to abandon the very concepts we use to understand ourselves.
