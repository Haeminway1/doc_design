The "Matthew effect," a term coined by sociologist Robert K. Merton, describes the phenomenon of cumulative advantage, where those who are already advantaged tend to gain more, while those who are disadvantaged fall further behind. The name comes from a passage in the Gospel of Matthew about the rich getting richer. In academia, for example, a well-known scientist is more likely to receive credit for a collaborative work than a lesser-known colleague. This recognition, in turn, enhances the famous scientist's reputation and access to future funding and opportunities, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of success. ________________________________________. It reveals how initial inequalities, whether in wealth, status, or recognition, can become amplified over time, not necessarily due to differences in merit, but because of the way social systems are structured to reward early advantage.
