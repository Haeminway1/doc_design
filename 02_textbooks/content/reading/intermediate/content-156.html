The "hermeneutics of suspicion" is a phrase coined by Paul Ricœur to describe a mode of interpretation common to the work of Marx, Nietzsche, and Freud. This interpretive style approaches a text or cultural phenomenon with a fundamental skepticism. ① It assumes that the surface-level or apparent meaning is a mask, concealing a deeper, hidden reality of economic interest, will to power, or repressed desire. ② The task of the critic, therefore, is to demystify, to unmask, and to expose the hidden forces at play. ③ This approach has been incredibly influential in modern critical theory, fostering a sharp and critical eye for ideology and power structures. ④ The hermeneutics of suspicion ultimately aims to restore and affirm the traditional, face-value meaning of a text. ⑤ It is a practice of "reading against the grain," seeking out the contradictions and hidden agendas that the text itself might try to conceal.
