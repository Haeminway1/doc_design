The "Sapir-Whorf hypothesis" posits a relationship between the language a person speaks and how they perceive and conceptualize the world. The "strong" version, known as linguistic determinism, claims that language entirely dictates thought. This version is now largely discredited. The "weak" version, linguistic relativity, is more widely accepted. It suggests that language doesn't lock us into a worldview but does influence our thought patterns and what we pay attention to. For example, a language that has distinct terms for different shades of blue may enable its speakers to perceive and recall those shades more easily than speakers of a language that has only one term for blue. This does not mean that the latter speakers cannot see the differences, but that their language does not habitually lead them to focus on them.
