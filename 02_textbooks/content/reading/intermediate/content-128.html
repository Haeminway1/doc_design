The philosophical movement of Stoicism, which flourished in ancient Greece and Rome, offered a comprehensive system for achieving *eudaimonia*, or a state of human flourishing and tranquility. ① The Stoics taught that virtue is the only true good and that external things like health, wealth, and reputation are morally indifferent. ② A central practice of Stoicism is the "dichotomy of control," which involves distinguishing between what is within our power (our judgments, impulses, and attitudes) and what is not (external events, the actions of others). ③ By focusing our energy solely on what we can control and accepting what we cannot with equanimity, we can achieve a state of inner peace, or *apatheia*, that is immune to the vicissitudes of fortune. ④ Epicureanism, another major Hellenistic philosophy, also sought tranquility but defined it as the absence of pain and fear, advocating for a life of simple pleasures. ⑤ This practical and resilient philosophy has seen a significant resurgence in modern times as a guide for cultivating mental fortitude in the face of life's challenges.

The "linguistic turn" in 20th-century philosophy marked a significant shift in the focus and methodology of the discipline. ① This movement was predicated on the belief that many traditional philosophical problems are not genuine metaphysical issues but are, in fact, confusions arising from a misunderstanding of the logic of our language. ② Philosophers like Ludwig Wittgenstein, a pivotal figure in this turn, argued that the task of philosophy is not to discover new truths about the world but to achieve clarity by analyzing how language is used. ③ For example, a debate about the nature of "time" might be dissolved by examining the different ways we use the word "time" in various contexts, rather than by searching for a single, essential entity. ④ The philosophy of mind, a related field, investigates the nature of consciousness and its relationship to the physical body. ⑤ Thus, the linguistic turn reoriented philosophy toward a therapeutic role, aiming to untangle the conceptual knots created by the misuse of language.
