The concept of "hegemony," as developed by the Marxist philosopher Antonio Gramsci, offers a nuanced explanation for the stability of capitalist societies. ① Gramsci argued that the ruling class maintains its dominance not primarily through overt force or coercion, but through a more subtle form of ideological and cultural leadership. ② This hegemony is achieved when the values, beliefs, and worldview of the ruling class become so deeply embedded in society that they are accepted as "common sense" by the subordinate classes. ③ This process is exercised through civil society institutions like the media, the education system, and religious organizations. ④ The theory of the leisure class, proposed by Thorstein Veblen, critiques the conspicuous consumption of the wealthy. ⑤ Consequently, the subordinate classes actively consent to their own domination, making a violent revolution less likely and the existing social order appear natural and inevitable.

The "affect heuristic" is a mental shortcut in which people rely on their immediate emotional reactions ("affect") to make judgments and decisions. ① Instead of engaging in a detailed, rational analysis of risks and benefits, we often consult our gut feelings. ② If we have a positive feeling about an activity, we tend to judge its risks as low and its benefits as high; if we have a negative feeling, we do the opposite. ③ For example, our affection for a particular brand or product might lead us to overlook its flaws and overestimate its advantages. ④ This reliance on emotion can be an efficient way to navigate a complex world, but it can also lead to systematic biases and poor choices, especially when our feelings are manipulated. ⑤ Prospect theory, another key concept in behavioral economics, describes how people make choices between probabilistic alternatives that involve risk.
