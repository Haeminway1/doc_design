The "hedonic treadmill," or hedonic adaptation, is a theory positing that people repeatedly return to a relatively stable baseline level of happiness, despite major life events. A positive event, like winning the lottery, may cause a temporary spike in happiness, but the individual's expectations and desires soon adapt to this new reality. The new standard of living becomes the norm, and the initial joy fades. Similarly, a negative event, such as a serious injury, causes a sharp drop in happiness, but people often show remarkable resilience and gradually adapt, returning to a level of happiness close to their previous baseline. This theory suggests that the pursuit of happiness through external circumstances is often a futile chase. Lasting changes in well-being, it implies, may be more dependent on internal factors, such as intentional activities, mindset, and social connections.

The concept of "biomimicry" is an approach to innovation that seeks sustainable solutions to human challenges by emulating nature's time-tested patterns and strategies. The core idea is that nature, after billions of years of evolution, has already solved many of the problems we are grappling with. For instance, engineers have created more efficient fan blades by studying the shape of whale flippers and developed stronger materials by mimicking the structure of abalone shells. Architects have designed self-cooling buildings inspired by the ventilation systems of termite mounds. This discipline represents a paradigm shift from an industrial model of "heat, beat, and treat" to a more ecological approach. It is not about simply copying nature's forms, but understanding the principles behind them. Thus, biomimicry advocates for learning from nature as a mentor, not just as a source of raw materials.
