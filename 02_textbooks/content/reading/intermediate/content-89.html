The philosophical inquiry into consciousness has been significantly reframed by David Chalmers' distinction between the "easy problems" and the "hard problem." The easy problems, while technically complex, are considered tractable within the current paradigms of cognitive science and neuroscience. These include explaining functions such as the integration of information, the reportability of mental states, and the control of behavior. (A)____________, the hard problem is the question of why and how any of this physical processing gives rise to subjective experience, or "qualia"—the qualitative feel of what it is like to see red or feel pain. This problem seems to resist conventional methods of explanation, as it is not a problem about objective functions but about first-person phenomenology. (B)____________, the distinction does not imply that the easy problems are trivial, but rather that they belong to a different category of explanation than the hard problem. This reframing challenges physicalist accounts of the mind to address the phenomenal aspect of consciousness directly.

The proposal to officially designate the current geological epoch as the "Anthropocene" reflects a growing recognition of humanity's profound and lasting impact on the Earth system. This designation moves beyond a metaphorical statement to a formal stratigraphic claim, suggesting that human activities have left a geologically significant mark on the planet's rock layers.
