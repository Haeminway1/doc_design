The "halo effect" is a cognitive bias where our overall impression of a person influences our judgments about their specific traits. A single positive characteristic can "radiate" or spread to color our perception of all their other characteristics. For example, a person who is perceived as physically attractive is often also assumed to be more intelligent, kind, and successful than someone who is less attractive, even in the absence of any evidence for these other qualities. This bias works in reverse as well; a single negative trait can create a "horn effect," causing us to assume a person has other negative qualities. This mental shortcut is powerful in many contexts, from job interviews to celebrity culture. It demonstrates our tendency to make quick, holistic judgments rather than engaging in a more effortful, trait-by-trait analysis.
