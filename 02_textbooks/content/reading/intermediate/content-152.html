The "liar paradox" is a classic semantic paradox that has troubled philosophers for centuries. It is most famously expressed in the sentence: "This statement is false." (A) If the statement is true, then what it says must be the case, which means the statement must be false. (B) If the statement is false, then what it says is not the case, which means the statement must be true. (C) The sentence thus appears to be both true and false, and neither true nor false, violating the law of non-contradiction and the law of the excluded middle. (D) This paradox reveals that natural language is perfectly logical and free from any self-referential problems. (E) Proposed solutions have ranged from creating hierarchical language levels (Alfred Tarski) to developing paraconsistent logics that can tolerate some contradictions.

The "observer-expectancy effect" is a form of cognitive bias where a researcher's cognitive bias causes them to subconsciously influence the participants of an experiment. This is distinct from deliberate fraud and often happens unintentionally. ① The researcher, holding a certain expectation about the outcome, may subtly alter their behavior, tone of voice, or how they record data in a way that steers the results toward their hypothesis. ② To mitigate this effect, double-blind experimental designs are often employed. ③ In such a design, neither the participants nor the individuals administering the experiment know who belongs to the control group and who belongs to the experimental group. ④ This ensures that the researcher's expectations can be more efficiently communicated to the participants, thus strengthening the results. ⑤ By preventing this knowledge from influencing the interaction, the double-blind method aims to produce more objective and reliable data.
