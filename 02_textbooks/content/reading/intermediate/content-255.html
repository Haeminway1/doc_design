The "affective fallacy" is a term from literary criticism, coined by W. K. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley, to describe the error of evaluating a work of art by its emotional effect on the reader. As key figures in the school of New Criticism, they argued that a poem is an autonomous object whose meaning should be derived from its formal properties—its structure, imagery, and wordplay—not from the subjective and variable psychological response it produces. ( ① ) To judge a poem as "good" because it makes one feel sad is, in their view, to commit this fallacy. ( ② ) This perspective insists on a clear separation between the text and its emotional consequences. ( ③ ) It sought to make literary criticism a more objective and rigorous discipline. ( ④ ) Reader-response criticism, which emerged later, argued that the reader's interaction with the text is central to the creation of meaning. ( ⑤ ) This approach aimed to ground interpretation in the text itself, free from the "relativism" of individual feelings.

The New Critics' argument against the "affective fallacy" is based on the belief that:
  • (1) a literary text is an autonomous object with objective properties.
  • (2) the reader's emotional response is the most important aspect of a text.
  • (3) all readers have the exact same emotional response to a given text.
  • (4) the author's biography is the key to understanding a text.
  • (5) literary criticism should be a subjective and impressionistic activity.
