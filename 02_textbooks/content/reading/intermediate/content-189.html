The "free rider problem" describes a market failure that occurs when those who benefit from a resource, good, or service do not pay for it, which results in an under-provision of those goods. This problem is common with "public goods," which are defined as being non-excludable and non-rivalrous. National defense is a classic example. It is impossible to exclude any citizen from its protection, and one person's protection does not diminish another's. Because of this, individuals have an incentive to avoid contributing to the cost of national defense, assuming others will pay for it. If too many people act as "free riders," however, the service will be inadequately funded or not funded at all. This dilemma is why such goods are typically funded through compulsory taxation rather than voluntary contributions.

The "just-world hypothesis" is a cognitive bias where a person's tendency to believe that the world is just and that people get what they deserve. This belief can be a comforting coping mechanism, as it implies a predictable and controllable world where good is rewarded and evil is punished. However, it has a significant dark side. It can lead to a tendency to blame the victims of misfortune for their own fate. For example, a believer in a just world might rationalize that a victim of assault must have done something to provoke the attack, or that a person in poverty is lazy. This rationalization protects the believer's worldview from the uncomfortable truth that terrible things can happen to good people randomly. Thus, the bias can inhibit empathy and a clear understanding of systemic injustice.
