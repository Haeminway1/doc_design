The "actant," a concept from Actor-Network Theory (ANT), represents a radical departure from traditional sociology's focus on human actors. ANT, developed by thinkers like Bruno Latour, proposes to flatten the distinction between human and non-human entities in a social analysis. An actant is any entity—human or non-human—that can be shown to act or to make a difference in a network of relations. Thus, a scientist, a microbe, a laboratory instrument, a scientific article, and a government funding agency can all be considered actants in the production of a scientific fact. The theory does not claim that a microbe has "intention" in the human sense. The concept of "parapraxis," commonly known as a "Freudian slip," is a key element in Sigmund Freud's psychoanalytic theory. Freud argued that errors in speech, memory, or physical action are not random accidents but are the result of unconscious wishes, conflicts, or thoughts breaking through into conscious behavior. A slip of the tongue, the temporary forgetting of a name, or the misplacing of an object are all seen as meaningful phenomena. They are compromises, where the conscious intention is disrupted by a repressed, unconscious intention. For example, a person might "accidentally" say the name of a former lover instead of their current partner's, revealing an unresolved unconscious attachment.
