The "Great Vowel Shift" was a massive series of sound changes in the history of the English language that affected the long vowels. It occurred primarily between the 15th and 18th centuries and marks a major transition from Middle English to Modern English. In this systematic shift, all the long vowels were "raised," meaning the tongue was positioned higher in the mouth to pronounce them. The highest long vowels, /iː/ (as in Middle English "meet") and /uː/ (as in "boot"), could not be raised further and instead became diphthongs, eventually evolving into the /aɪ/ (as in modern "bite") and /aʊ/ (as in "out") sounds. ________________________________________. For example, the word "name" was once pronounced with a long "ah" sound, but the shift raised it to the "ay" sound we use today. This historical change is the primary reason for the notorious inconsistencies between English spelling and pronunciation.
