The political philosopher Giorgio Agamben has developed a highly influential theory of sovereignty and biopolitics, centered on the concept of "bare life" (*homo sacer*). ( ① ) He begins by analyzing the ancient Roman figure of *homo sacer*, a person who could be killed by anyone with impunity but could not be ritually sacrificed. ( ② ) This figure, for Agamben, represents a life that is included in the political order only through its exclusion—a life that can be taken but is not recognized as politically significant. ( ③ ) He argues that the sovereign's power is fundamentally defined by the ability to declare a state of exception, a legal suspension of the law, where citizens can be reduced to this state of bare life. ( ④ ) It is a zone of indistinction between law and violence, where the protections of citizenship are stripped away. ( ⑤ ) This is evident in phenomena like the concentration camp or the legal status of "enemy combatants."

The anthropologist Clifford Geertz advocated for an "interpretive" approach to the study of culture. ( ① ) He famously argued that culture is not a power or a system that causes social action, but a context within which it can be intelligibly described. ( ② ) The task of the ethnographer, therefore, is not to create universal laws of human behavior but to understand and interpret the web of significance that people themselves have spun. ( ③ ) He illustrated this with the example of a wink. ( ④ ) A simple, "thin" description would report the rapid contraction of an eyelid. ( ⑤ ) It would distinguish between an involuntary twitch, a conspiratorial signal to a friend, a parody of that signal, or a rehearsal of the parody, all of which look physically identical but have vastly different social meanings.
