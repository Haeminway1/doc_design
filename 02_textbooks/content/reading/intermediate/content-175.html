The "tessellation" of a plane is the covering of a surface with one or more geometric shapes, called tiles, with no overlaps and no gaps. While seemingly simple, the mathematical principles governing tessellations are quite strict. For a regular tessellation, which uses only one type of regular polygon, only three shapes will work: the equilateral triangle, the square, and the regular hexagon. This is because the internal angle of the polygon at each vertex must be a divisor of 360 degrees to allow the shapes to meet perfectly at a point. For example, the 120-degree internal angle of a hexagon allows three hexagons to meet precisely at a vertex (3 x 120 = 360). A regular pentagon, with its 108-degree angle, cannot tile the plane without leaving gaps. These principles extend to more complex semi-regular and non-periodic tilings, revealing a deep connection between geometry and order.

The "nocebo effect" is the malevolent twin of the placebo effect, where negative expectations about a treatment can cause harmful outcomes. If a patient is told a medication may cause nausea, they may begin to feel nauseous even if they are taking an inert sugar pill. This is not a matter of simple imagination; the negative belief can trigger real physiological responses, such as the release of stress hormones. This phenomenon creates a profound ethical dilemma in clinical practice. The principle of informed consent requires doctors to warn patients of potential risks and side effects. However, this very act of warning can plant a negative suggestion in the patient's mind, thereby inducing nocebo-related harm. This tension forces clinicians to navigate a difficult path between ethical transparency and the potential for iatrogenic harm.
