The "rhizomatic" learning model, inspired by the philosophy of Deleuze and Guattari, offers a radical alternative to traditional, hierarchical models of education. In a traditional "arborescent" (tree-like) model, knowledge is transmitted from an expert (the root) to a novice (the branches) in a linear, predetermined curriculum. The rhizomatic model, in contrast, sees learning as a network with no fixed center or pre-established path. The curriculum is not a set path to be followed but a map to be explored and added to. Knowledge is co-constructed by the community of learners, who bring their own connections and experiences to the table. In this model, the role of the teacher shifts from being an expert dispenser of content to being a facilitator and co-learner. The "ideological state apparatuses" (ISAs), a concept from Louis Althusser, are the institutions that reproduce the dominant ideology through non-violent means. Althusser distinguishes the ISAs from the "repressive state apparatus" (RSA), which is the army, police, and courts that function primarily by force. The ISAs include institutions like the family, the education system, the media, and religious organizations. While the RSA belongs to the public domain, the ISAs are largely in the private domain. However, both serve the ultimate function of securing the reproduction of the relations of production. The RSA achieves this through coercion, while the ISAs achieve it through ideology. In capitalist societies, Althusser argues, the dominant ISA is the educational apparatus, which instills the values and skills necessary to be a compliant and productive member of the workforce.
